U.S. Manufacturing Gained Strength in June
By Michael S. Derby and John McAuley and Rebecca Christie
761 words
2 July 2002
The Asian Wall Street Journal
AWSJ
A2
English
(Copyright (c) 2002, Dow Jones & Company, Inc.)

NEW YORK -- U.S. manufacturing activity increased for a fifth straight month in June -- and at a faster pace than the previous month -- boosted by higher exports related to the weaker dollar and amid signs of increasing price pressures, according to a closely watched survey.

Separately, the Commerce Department said U.S. construction spending fell during May, amid declines in residential and commercial construction.

The Institute for Supply Management said its purchasing managers' index rose to 56.2 in June from 55.7 for May and 53.9 in April. Economists had expected the factory sector to have grown at about the same pace as the prior month. A reading above 50 indicates expansion of activity in the manufacturing sector, while a reading below 50 denote contraction.

"The growth in manufacturing activity accelerated slightly during June," said Norbert Ore, who directs the survey for the group, formerly known as the National Association of Purchasing Management. "Steel-price increases are drawing a lot of attention from supply managers, and while energy prices have moderated, they are still a concern."

Price pressures rose for the fourth-straight month, amid increases in steel costs, with the ISM's price index rising to 65.5 from 63.0 in May.

The report said the weakening dollar has been an "immediate" benefit for U.S. manufacturing exports. June's reading of 56.2, if it were maintained, corresponds with 4.9% annualized growth in U.S. gross domestic product.

The group's new-orders index continued to grow, although not at May's strong pace. The orders index stood at 60.8 in June, after surging to 63.1 in May.

Employment in the manufacturing sector remained tepid, with a reading of 49.7 following May's 47.3. Inventories, the rebuilding of which helped drive first-quarter U.S. growth, continued to fall. The June inventories index stood at 43.7, down from 45.6 in May.

The manufacturing report comes amid other data that suggest the U.S. economy's overall growth rate has cooled from the strong pace of the first quarter. The Federal Reserve said last week at the end of its policy meeting that "growth in final demand has moderated" recently, although it expects "the rate of increase of final demand to pick up over coming quarters."

The gradual and still-vulnerable nature of the recovery has moved many economists to push back their estimates of when the Fed will raise interest rates this year. Many expect the central bank to hold off until November before lifting its overnight federal-funds target rate. Still, with the dismal state of equities markets, and the as-yet unseen pickup in business investment still hanging in the balance, some believe the Fed may be able to wait out the entire year before raising rates.

The Commerce Department's report showed spending on residential construction retreated in May from April's record high.

Total construction spending fell 0.7% in May to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of $852 billion. April construction spending was revised to a 0.4% increase from an original report of a 0.2% rise.

Economists surveyed by Dow Jones, on average, were expecting construction spending to rise 0.3% in May.

Private construction expenditures fell 1.5% in May after a 0.4% rise in April. Within that category, residential construction spending fell 0.8% in May to a rate of $412.3 billion after rising 0.4% in April.

April's residential construction reached a record rate of $415.5 billion, despite a big downward revision. It had originally been estimated at a rate of $425.5 billion, a 0.7% gain from the month before.

The May fall in construction spending was muted by a 1.9% rise in public construction spending, which includes roads, sewer systems and schools.

The construction numbers follow strong housing-market data released recently. Sales of new, single-family homes rose to a record level in May, reflecting continued strength in the housing market. However, most economists believe the economy has cooled. Analysts believe the economy is growing at a 2.0% to 2.5% pace as stock prices have dropped and consumer confidence and spending have waned.

Spending on private nonresidential construction, such as office buildings, fell 3.1% in May after a 0.1% rise in the previous month.

Total construction spending was up 0.6% on a year-to-year basis.

The Economy: Declining Dollar Helps Recovery In Manufacturing --- June Production Index Rose To Highest Level in 3 Years; New Export Orders Climbed
By Greg Ip
682 words
2 July 2002
The Wall Street Journal
J
A2
English
(Copyright (c) 2002, Dow Jones & Company, Inc.)

WASHINGTON -- The falling dollar is boosting the recovery in manufacturing, which gathered speed in June.

The Institute of Supply Management's index of manufacturing activity rose to 56.2 in June from 55.7 in May, its fifth consecutive month over 50, the level above which manufacturing expands. The June sub-index of production, one of the components of the overall index, at 61.4, was the highest in three years and suggests the industrial recovery is gathering speed.

The positive news on manufacturing contrasts with recent reports of weak retail sales in May and declining consumer confidence in June, as well as the stock market's recent dismal performance. Those developments had raised concerns that the recovery from last year's recession was in danger. Yesterday's report, based on a survey of manufacturing purchasing managers, suggests the opposite, at least in the industrial sector: that the recovery is accelerating.

The weaker dollar is contributing to that recovery, as the index of new export orders rose to 54.5, a two-year high, from 53.3. Norbert Ore, chairman of the ISM's manufacturing survey committee and director of strategic sourcing and procurement at Georgia-Pacific Corp., said the dollar's importance was especially apparent in purchasing managers' remarks on their survey responses.

A textile maker wrote: "The euro seems to be gaining ground on the dollar, our orders are up about 25%." A chemicals company reported: "Weaker dollar, stronger orders." Finally, a manager at a capital-equipment producer said, "With dollar changes taking place, we have now become more competitive in manufacturing as compared to our European facilities."

The trade-weighted dollar has dropped 5% since the end of February, although it is still up 39% from 1995. It has declined amid foreigners' disenchantment with U.S. economic policies, corporate-governance scandals and concern about the pace of recovery.

Although wholesale selling of U.S. stocks and bonds could drive up interest rates and push down stocks, hurting the recovery, so far that doesn't appear to have happened. Indeed, economists at J.P. Morgan Chase & Co. said the dollar's decline will boost margins on U.S. companies' foreign sales, "reinforcing a [profits] revival that is already under way.

Companies with overseas exposures could be offering upside guidance in the coming weeks as they prepare to report second-quarter earnings." Yesterday, 3M Co., which sells products such as Post-It Notes and Scotch Tape world-wide, said it would earn more than Wall Street expected in the second quarter, in part, one analyst said, because the dollar's strength was less of a problem than in previous quarters.

Also providing a tail wind for manufacturers is customers' need to rebuild inventories that were deeply depleted over the recession because many companies filled orders with products already on warehouse shelves.

The June purchasing-managers' survey found the inventory reduction continued but at a slower rate, and respondents said customers' stockpiles were still too low.

Separately, the Commerce Department said construction spending fell 0.7% in May. Residential construction retreated from April's all-time high, while spending on private nonresidential construction, such as office buildings, tumbled 3.1%.

Strong consumer incomes and low mortgage rates have generally buoyed residential construction, while rising office vacancies and weak corporate profits have pummeled nonresidential building. Public construction spending, which includes roads, sewer systems and schools, rose 1.9%.

U.S. Posts Firm Economic Data --- May Orders for Big-Ticket Items Rose 0.6%; New Home Sales Jumped 8.1% --- Single-Family Housing Report Surpasses Expectations of Analysts in a Survey
By Jennifer Corbett Dooren and Elizabeth Price
504 words
27 June 2002
The Asian Wall Street Journal
AWSJ
A2
English
(Copyright (c) 2002, Dow Jones & Company, Inc.)

WASHINGTON -- Orders for big-ticket items rose in May, the fifth rise in six months.

Separately, sales of new, single-family homes rose to a record level in May, reflecting continued strength in the housing market.

Orders for durable goods -- items meant to last three years or longer -- rose 0.6% to $173.17 billion last month, the U.S. Commerce Department reported Wednesday. April durable-goods orders were reduced to a 0.4% gain after previously being estimated as a 1.1% increase. March durable-goods orders also were reduced to a 0.3% decline after previously being estimated as a 0.3% increase.

The May figure was in line with Wall Street's expectations. A Dow Jones Newswires-CNBC survey of economists predicted a 0.5% rise for the month.

The May durable-goods report showed gains across most categories, except defense, machinery and cars.

Orders for transportation equipment rose by 0.5%, as aircraft orders jumped by 49.2%. Orders for cars and parts, however, fell by 2%. If transportation orders were excluded, durable-goods orders would have increased 0.6%.

Defense capital goods orders were off by 9.7% after a 46.6% plunge in April. If defense orders were excluded from overall durable-goods orders, orders would have increased 0.8% for the month.

The report showed that businesses have started making larger investments again. But there also were some bright economic numbers released yesterday. U.S. expenditures on new office buildings, roads, public-works projects and homes rose for the second consecutive month in November.

Total construction spending grew 0.8% to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of $865.1 billion, the Commerce Department said. That was mostly a result of a strong gain in public construction spending. October construction spending was revised to a 0.8% increase from the 1.9% rise previously estimated.

Until October, construction spending had fallen for five straight months as the overall U.S. economy weakened. The U.S. has been in a recession since March.

The November construction numbers were almost exactly in line with Wall Street's expectations. A consensus forecast by Thomson Financial called for construction expenditures to rise by 0.9% for the month.

But the increase in jobless claims for last week was greater than Wall Street's expectations. A Dow Jones Newswires-CNBC survey of five economists predicted claims would rise by 6,000 to 398,000.

The four-week moving average, which smooths out weekly fluctuations, fell for the fourth straight week to the lowest level since September. The four-week moving average was 409,750, a decline from the 418,000 level recorded in the week that ended Dec. 22.

The unemployment rate for workers with unemployment insurance remained at 2.9%, as the total number of workers drawing unemployment benefits rose by 42,000 to 3,715,000 during the week that ended Dec. 22.

Although layoffs have been declining in recent weeks, economists expect the unemployment rate to rise until the middle of this year. The rate, 5.7% in November, is expected to rise above 6% by then, economists said.

Virginia reported the largest increase in initial claims for unemployment benefits for the week that ended Dec. 22, saying that claims rose by 9,576. Michigan reported an increase of 7,925 claims, which it attributed to layoffs in the automobile industry.

Economic Indicators Give Hope to Recession's End --- Progress Is Seen in Housing, Consumer Confidence, Spending by Businesses
By Robert Gavin

Business investment, housing and consumer confidence all showed surprising and widespread improvement, adding to the evidence that an end to the recession could be in sight.

In a wave of economic indicators released Friday, orders for new capital equipment rose in November after a similar gain in October -- a hopeful sign since slumping business spending has been a dominant characteristic of the current recession. Other positives included a big jump in consumer confidence in December, stronger sales of both new and existing homes in November and a third straight week of relatively slow growth in unemployment-insurance claims.

"What we're beginning to see more and more is that the sharpest contractions are over, and we're starting to build for an economic recovery in the spring," said Steven A. Wood, chief economist for FinancialOxygen Inc., a financial-services firm based in Walnut Creek, Calif.

Orders for durable goods, which are designed to last three or more years, fell 4.8% in November, the Commerce Department said, after surging a record 12.5% in October. But that swing was dominated by defense orders. In October, early bookings associated with the $200 billion Joint Strike Fighter contract to Lockheed Martin Corp., Bethesda, Md., helped defense orders more than triple in October. In November, they fell back, plummeting 70%. Excluding the volatile defense sector, November orders were up for the second consecutive month, rising 2.7% on top of October's 4.8% gain.

Orders for nondefense capital goods, a crucial indicator of business investment, rose 4.8% in November, after increasing 5.9% in October. Technology orders were up 2%; motor vehicles rose 4.5%; electrical equipment increased 2.6%; and machinery gained 2.4%. Additionally, inventories fell 1.1% in November, leading Thomas J. Duesterberg, president and chief executive of the Manufacturers Alliance, Arlington, Va., to predict a recovery by spring. "Inventories have been pared back so much that production is likely to pick up even more as we enter the new year," he said.

Many analysts, however, noted that civilian durable-goods orders remained below the pre-Sept. 11 pace, and they said a rebound may not be imminent. "It's still too early to know whether the manufacturing sector is bouncing back for real," Mark Vitner, economist for Wachovia Securities, said in a report.

Nonetheless, economists noted other positive signs. The housing market continues to defy the recession, helped by low interest rates and mild weather in much of the nation. The average fixed rate for a 30-year mortgage rate was 6.66% in November, a point lower than the average rate a year earlier. The National Association of Realtors reported Friday that existing-home sales could possibly break the record of 5.21 million units set in 1999. November sales matched that pace, the association said, up 0.6% from a 5.18 million pace in October. New-home sales rose to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 934,000 units, up 6.4% from 878,000 in October, the Commerce Department reported.

Meantime, the Conference Board's index of consumer confidence jumped to 93.7 this month from 84.9 in November, putting it near pre-Sept. 11 levels. The increase was in line with results of the University of Michigan's index of consumer sentiment, which jumped to 88.8 this month from 83.9 in November. The Conference Board said the December rise was driven by a sharp jump in the expectations index, which measures consumers' feelings about the future.

The improvement in consumer sentiment could be the product of a stabilizing job market. Initial claims for unemployment benefits rose 7,000 last week to 392,000, the Labor Department said. It was the third consecutive week that new jobless claims remained below 400,000, the level above which some analysts say overall employment starts to contract, and significantly below the 535,000 new claims reported for the last week of September. Meantime, the four-week moving average, a less volatile measure, continued to drop, to 413,000, from 439,000 the week before. That is the lowest level since Sept. 15.
