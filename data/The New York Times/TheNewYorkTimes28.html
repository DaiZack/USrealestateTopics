WASHINGTON, Dec. 18 -- Home builders broke ground last month on the largest number of projects since July as low mortgage rates and mild weather helped the housing market remain a bright spot in the economy, the Commerce Department reported today.

It said that housing construction rose to a seasonally adjusted annual pace of 1.65 million housing units in November, an 8.2 percent increase from figures in October.

The rise was the biggest one-month gain since January and pushed housing starts to the highest level since July, when they reached an annual pace of 1.66 million units.

November's rebound came after housing starts fell by a revised 4 percent in October, a larger drop than the government previously reported.

The stronger-than-expected performance came even with consumer confidence at a seven-and-a-half-year low, unemployment worsening to 5.7 percent and payrolls falling.

Low mortgage rates are a crucial reason that the housing and construction markets have remained stable as the national economy is in recession.

The average interest rate on a 30-year, fixed-rate mortgage was 6.7 percent in November, compared with 7.7 percent a year earlier. Early in November, the mortgage company Freddie Mac reported, 30-year fixed-rate mortgages dipped to 6.45 percent, near a record low in 30 years of record-keeping. They have since risen, to 7.09 percent last week.

''Home buyers likely rushed to take advantage of low rates before they moved higher,'' said Karen Dexter, an economist at Merrill Lynch. ''Warm weather also contributed to the jump.''

The construction of single-family homes rose 3.2 percent in November, to an annual pace of 1.26 million units, after a 3.4 percent decline the month before. The building of apartments, condominiums and other such housing soared by 30.1 percent last month, to an annual 346,000, after a 2.9 percent drop in October.

New Jersey residential real estate brokers and agents are so energetically upbeat about the direction of sales prices in the spring that the image of a new chorus line for ''The Producers'' leaps to mind: high-kicking realtors belting out ''Springtime for real estate in New Jersey!''

Both the retail and the commercial markets may be showing signs of slowing, but residential home sales? Ah, the fresh, sweet smell of rising dollar volume after a weak winter.

At Weichert Realtors, ''we came off the best May on record, and we are paced for the best June on record,'' said Jacelyn Botti, a senior vice president. ''I've been doing real estate for five years,'' said Betty Anne McKenna, a Coldwell Banker saleswoman in Monmouth County, ''and this is the busiest summer I've had. Eighteen people or couples showed up, and one couple, Steve and Sandy Kaplan, had signed a contract to buy it for $525,000 by Monday evening.

Almost uniformly, realtors say that home buyers who may have waited anxiously while the stock market gyrated earlier this year are now shopping with gusto.

''This is strictly a cyclical business, with value entirely based on consumer confidence,'' Mr. Schweppe said, ''and consumer confidence is going down.''

Not because of the shaky stock market, Mr. Schweppe insisted, but because home values are inflated due to the tight inventory and because overpricing feeds on itself.

When there are fewer homes to sell, fierce buyer competition and multiple bids become commonplace in towns like Montclair, where homes are generally in good condition, schools are good and the commute to New York City is short. ''Somebody steps up to bat every time a desirable home comes on the market and pays whatever it takes,'' Mr. Schweppe said.

Ms. Botti of Weichert says that only a few years ago, when a buyer's agent found out that multiple offers were being made, the agent would suggest that the client offer 10 percent over the list price. ''Now,'' Ms. Botti said, ''you really have to extend yourself.''

In certain towns, there are cases of people bidding $100,000 or more over asking price and still losing out, according to Mr. Schweppe, Ms. Botti and others.

''The buildup is happening, and it's because we've overpriced the product. You can see it all over, even in the so-called safe towns like Montclair, and Short Hills, and Chatham, with houses sitting on the market for two and three months.''

Coldwell Banker's president, Mrs. Passerini, says the market is robust in most places. She cited the case of a young New York City couple looking for a house in Mountain Lakes, where the man grew up.

''They found a nice colonial with a hot tub, very unusual in Mountain Lakes. They outbid everyone and got the house. While the sale was undergoing the three-day attorney review -- and offers can still be accepted -- four more came in.''

Mrs. Passerini says that inventory will inevitably begin to grow and prices to drop but adds that the marketplace can remain strong because demand for houses is so pent up.

''We represent a lot of builders,'' she said, ''and this never happens. I've been doing this 27 years, but they are building $3 million to $4 million houses on spec. There are seven going up right now.''

Ms. Botti of Weichert, predicting ''no letup,'' continues to advise sellers to be very aggressive in setting list prices. Some appraisers are applying a certain percentage per month that a home will appreciate while the closing is pending when setting its value for a mortgage company, Ms. Botti says.

''We tell sellers, 'You should have the value of that appreciation up until closing, not the buyer,' '' she said. Ms. Botti says Weichert has also represented buyers who closed on a property, put it back on the market immediately and made thousands of dollars.

Ms. Reeves of Burgdorff says she cannot make predictions. ''Hey, this is a fun market to watch,'' she laughed. ''You can't really tell what will happen!''

SAN FRANCISCO, Aug. 1 -- At 21 years old, Shanika Long is giving up on San Francisco, the city she was born and reared in and would rather still call home.

Her mother tells her of a time when blacks owned certain parts of town, when the Fillmore District, for one, was a vibrant neighborhood with, she recalls, ''a black-power-type mentality.''

But, like the Fillmore's nickname, Harlem West, those days are history. And Ms. Long, a clerk at the Labor Ready agency for temporary workers who has a 3-year-old daughter, says she wants to live where black people can afford to buy houses and rear children.

''I'm moving out and I'm feeling like I'm being pushed out of San Francisco,'' she said. ''The community now is, like, dead.''

African-Americans, like Ms. Long, are leaving this city in droves. Over the last 10 years, as public housing and low-income projects have been torn down and as rents and house prices climbed to record levels, African-Americans have left San Francisco like no other city.

Census figures show that while the city's overall population increased more than 7 percent in the 1990's, the number of people who list their race as black fell from 79,039 in 1990 to 60,515 last year (with an additional 6,561 reporting some black heritage combined with another race, the first time the census allowed people to check a mixed-race category). That leaves the city of 776,733 with a black population of 8.6 percent.

Other cities have had notable declines in their black populations over the last decade -- Washington, for example.

But blacks in other cities appear to be migrating to the suburbs in a pattern of upward mobility. In San Francisco, many are leaving because they have no choice. Gentrification during the dot-com boom gave the city the distinction as the most expensive in the country.

Landlords in black neighborhoods, much like others, cashed in, raising rents and evicting long-term tenants. The recent technology bust has had little effect in lowering housing prices, real estate experts say. And since blacks have always been a relatively small minority here (13 percent of the population at its height in 1970) the consequences are striking.

The result, in one of the few major cities with a black mayor and a liberal political sensibility in sync with a majority of African-Americans, is a San Francisco with whole neighborhoods where it is rare to see a black person. It is a city where blacks have little clout, few cultural institutions and only one remaining neighborhood, the homely, lonely Bayview-Hunters Point, best known for a sewage treatment plant and radioactive Superfund site.

For many blacks here, San Francisco is the sweetheart who loved 'em and left 'em, who promised the moon and stars only to forget them when new blood came to town.

In the Fillmore, there has been much talk over the years of establishing a jazz district in honor of the jazz scene that emerged in the neighborhood from 1940 to 1950. It was the heyday of the community, when the black population of the city grew tenfold as thousands of blacks came to San Francisco looking for war jobs, many of them at the Navy shipyard at Hunters Point. But the jazz project has been in the planning stages for years with little action.

In 1997, Mayor Willie L. Brown Jr. promised to bring Bayview-Hunters Point thousands of jobs, a new stadium for the San Francisco 49ers and a megamall to go along with it. Voters approved the project in 1997, agreeing to finance it with $100 million in bonds. When Erica Hutchinson and her fiance, Lynn Corbett, bought their brownstone in the Clinton Hill section of Brooklyn for $175,000 three years ago, they persuaded a bank to lend them $230,000. Their argument was that the value of their new home would jump once they used the extra mortgage money to gut the three-story building and fill it with new bathrooms, a new kitchen, new flooring, new windows -- new everything.

They were right, of course. The Corbetts, now married, say that after several years of a flush New York economy their brownstone would easily sell today for at least $370,000. Their bankers seem to agree. The couple recently added a $30,000 home equity loan, using the new money to pay off credit card bills. ''The construction costs were more than we had anticipated so we charged them,'' said Ms. Corbett, who is 30, like her husband, ''and we paid for our wedding with credit cards, too.''

For most American families, their home is their biggest investment, and after years of prosperity in which home prices rose substantially, family wealth has increased as well. But like the Corbetts, millions of households borrowed heavily against their homes, often to help support greater spending.

The borrowing has been so extensive, in fact, that homeowners, after building up equity through much of the 1960's and 1970's, have let their ownership shares deteriorate over the last two decades to the lowest level on record, the Federal Reserve reports. The borrowing even accelerated in the 1990's, helping to explain how Americans managed to easily outspend their incomes through two long economic expansions.

Now a slowing economy catches the average household owning less of a stake in its home than in any economic slowdown since the advent of the modern mortgage in the 1930's. If a recession develops and people start worrying that they have too little equity left to continue borrowing safely against their homes, the blow to spending could turn a mild recession into a prolonged one. That would certainly happen, economists say, if a downturn causes home prices to fall, shrinking even more the equity stake that households still have in their homes.

''When you make borrowing dependent on your home, then you erode the fire wall that protects your standard of living and we are going into this slowdown with the fire wall clearly eroded,'' said Nicolas Retsinas, director of the Joint Center on Housing Studies at Harvard. ''The home is not just a financial transaction, it defines who you are and what kind of community you live in. When all of a sudden you get into trouble, you can't just rip it up like a credit card.''

The average homeowning household owed lenders 46 percent of the market value of its residence during last year's third quarter, up sharply from about 30 percent in 1982 and 40 percent in 1991, at the start of the current economic expansion, the Fed reports show. For a typical family with a home worth the median market value of $144,000 late last year, that meant their equity was $77,760 while their debt was $66,240.

That still sounds like a relatively safe cushion. But American society once exalted a different norm -- a 20 percent down payment and the balance paid off through a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage. The Champagne toasts and mortgage burnings that celebrated the final payment are gone today. Instead, a growing number of Americans are approaching or entering retirement still making hefty home payments.

Q What are the benefits and drawbacks of having a ''sit-down'' contract of sale when purchasing real estate? . . . George M. Colt, the Bronx.

A Peter Schwartz, a Manhattan real estate lawyer, said that in most residential real estate transactions, the contract of sale is the product of negotiations conducted between the lawyers for the parties rather than the parties themselves. In most cases, Mr. Schwartz said, after the buyer and seller agree on a price, the seller's lawyer draws up the contract and sends it to the buyer's lawyer.

The buyer's lawyer makes whatever changes he thinks necessary, allows the client to sign it, and then sends the amended contract back to the seller's lawyer for signing by the seller -- if the seller agrees to the amendments. In some cases, Mr. Schwartz said, the contract can be amended several times before its terms have been agreed to by all.

With a so-called sit-down contract, Mr. Schwartz said, the negotiations are conducted in one place, at one time and with all the parties and their lawyers present.

''Sit-downs usually happen when there are anxious parties on both sides who don't want to wait for their attorneys to draft the contract,'' he said.

The most obvious benefit of a sit-down negotiation, Mr. Schwartz said, is that the parties end up with a binding contract in the least amount of time.

''That's especially good for purchasers these days because in a seller's market, it makes sense for the buyer to lock the seller into the deal as quickly as possible,'' he said. At the same time, Mr. Schwartz said, the primary disadvantage of a sit-down contract is that it costs more.

''Usually, both lawyers will charge more because instead of doing the negotiations in the comfort of their office, one or both will have to go out to a meeting and then sit there while the parties try to hammer out a deal,'' he said.

Mr. Schwartz said that while a sit-down contract may be useful for the purchase of a house -- because most such contracts contain clauses allowing the buyer to cancel the deal if defects are revealed by a home inspection -- the same may not be said for purchases of an apartment.

''When you're buying an apartment, you always want your lawyer to conduct a due-diligence before you sign,'' he said, referring to the examination of books and records that lawyers conduct to ensure that a co-op corporation or condominium association is financially sound.

Dealing With A Judgment Lien

Q At a real estate closing, what is the normal way for the parties to deal with a judgment lien on the property? . . . Donna Raft, Manhattan.

A ''The normal way to deal with a judgment lien at a closing is to pay it,'' said John G. Hall, a Staten Island real estate lawyer.

Mr. Hall explained that a judgment lien is a legal filing that gives a creditor an interest in real estate owned by a debtor. And while such a lien is not filed against a specific piece of property, he said, the filing of such a lien typically makes it impossible for the debtor to sell a property in the county where the lien was filed until the lien is paid.

Accordingly, Mr. Hall said, if the title search reveals an outstanding lien against the seller, the seller's lawyer will contact the person who filed the lien to determine the amount necessary to pay it -- an amount that often includes interest that has accrued since the judgment was obtained.

If the figure is determined before the closing, he said, the lawyer for the buyer will satisfy it out of the proceeds of the sale that are due to the seller.

If the payoff amount of the lien is not known on the day of closing, Mr. Hall said, the title company for the buyer will generally require the buyer's lawyer to hold twice the amount of the original lien in escrow until the exact payoff amount is determined and the lien is paid in full and removed from official records.

''Most new-home buyers don't see the need for a home inspection,'' Mr. Austin said, adding that while construction codes are designed to ensure that new construction is completed in accordance with a set of minimum standards, the code-enforcement inspectors can't catch everything and aren't even looking for problems of an aesthetic nature.
