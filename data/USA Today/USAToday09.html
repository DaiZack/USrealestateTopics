Sales of newly built homes rose a surprising 3.4% from October to November, the Commerce Department said Wednesday, as more builders offered special deals to attract buyers, and buyers took advantage of declining interest rates and mild weather to visit model homes.

This is the third increase in four months and a sign that the housing correction may be ending. Builder confidence levels, which hit bottom in September, are also slowly beginning to rise, according to December's Housing Market Index.

The sales gains haven't been easy. About half of home builders say they are cutting prices or paying the buyers' closing costs, and almost 60% are offering free upgrades such as granite kitchen counters, according to a survey by the National Association of Home Builders.

Compared with November last year, sales were off 15%. And while December results are not yet in, sales of new homes for the year are expected to be down about 18% from 2005. Sales of existing homes should be off by almost 9% this year. The National Association of Realtors will release November's existing home sales today.

"This is very much like what we saw at the time of the 1990-91 recession, even though this is not a recession in economic terms," says David Seiders, chief economist for the NAHB. "It shows to what extent this market got overheated in 2004 and particularly 2005. We knew we had soared to unsustainable levels ... and knew we had one heck of a correction coming."

There are still signals that home sales could continue to slide. Applications for mortgage loans last week fell 14% from the week before to their lowest level in nearly five months, the Mortgage Bankers Association said Wednesday.

Nevertheless, sales of new homes were stronger last month in every region of the country except the South.

The supply of new homes for sale dipped to 6.3 months, down from 6.7 months in October. "Inventories fell again for a fourth- consecutive month," Wachovia analyst Phillip Neuhart says. "As long as they continue to fall, I'm going to feel better and better about the new home market."

The median price for a new home was $251,700, up 3% from October and 6% from last November. But Seiders dismissed the increase, saying it was probably because sales were strongest in the Northeast and the West, where median prices for homes are the highest.

The supply of affordable housing continued to dwindle this year despite weakening home prices, according to one-third of local housing officials who responded to a survey by the National League of Cities.

Three-fourths of those surveyed say housing costs are significantly limiting home ownership opportunities for lower- income residents and young families. The survey was sent to officials in 1,167 cities.

"People don't recognize the social costs of that," says James Hunt, president of the National League of Cities and a councilman in Clarksburg, W.Va. "In a lot of areas, people who can't find affordable housing are (commuting) two hours in their cars." He says cities are under pressure to address the shortage as federal aid declines, cutting into such programs as housing vouchers and community development grants.

Housing prices in many areas skyrocketed in recent years as interest rates dropped. Demand fueled teardowns and rehabilitation of older homes. That took cheaper housing off the market. Thousands of apartments were converted to condominiums, squeezing renters. A recent downturn in home prices in some areas hasn't offset those pressures.

Lower-income and middle-income working families -- including teachers and firefighters -- along with the elderly, the disabled and single parents face the most critical housing needs, according to local officials who responded to the League of Cities survey. The report is being released today at a conference in Reno.

Federal money for housing "has declined every single year that I've been in office," says Indianapolis Mayor Bart Peterson, who took office in 2000. "Tight funds have forced us to do creative things."

Indianapolis' Fall Creek Place district once was so downtrodden it was nicknamed Dodge City. About 75% of its housing was abandoned or torn down. In the late 1990s, the neighborhood got a $4 million federal grant to boost home ownership. The city leveraged the money to attract private investment and pitched in almost $20 million to help homebuyers make down payments. Fifty homes were rehabilitated and more than 400 built. Most were sold below market rates and are owned by low-income, first-time buyers.

"We can now do this without the assistance of the federal government," Peterson says. "We'd love to have it, but we're not getting it, so we have to figure out a way to do it ourselves."

Some cities are devoting tax revenue to housing. Others require that developments include affordable units. "If we're ... going to make a difference, we can't look to outside help," Hunt says. "We need to make a substantial investment of city dollars."
