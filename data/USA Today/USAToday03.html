Much of Los Angeles' history can be explored in the heart of downtown, dating back to its founding in 1781. Within a compact area, you can get a big picture of the city's storied history that includes electric streetcars, cable railroads, flappers and several brushes with Hollywood. Learning about the significant landmarks and architectural styles can turn buildings you might otherwise overlook into noteworthy sites that enrich your visit. The Los Angeles Conservancy highlights many of these during historical walking tours.

1. One of the most photographed buildings in the city, the Bradbury Building is a national historic landmark and the oldest commercial building in the downtown area. Built in 1893 for gold mining and real estate magnate Lewis Bradbury, the Bradbury Building has an iron and wood interior, with a large skylight that was vital in the days before electricity. Bradbury didn't live to see the completion of his namesake building, finished the year after his death. Today the building is used as office space, and has many of its original elements intact including open-cage elevators, wall sconces and an iron mailbox. The building continues to be a popular filming destination for movie, television and music video sets, including blockbusters such as Blade Runner and 500 Days of Summer.

2. Designated by the city as a historic-cultural monument, the PacMutual Building was built in 1908 as the new headquarters of The Pacific Mutual Life Insurance Company. After an earthquake damaged its home office in San Francisco, the company relocated to Los Angeles where they constructed the Beaux Arts building using terra-cotta and reinforced concrete. The building was meant to conjure feelings of strength, dependability and security to visiting clients purchasing life insurance policies. After exiting a streetcar and entering the PacMutual building, visitors were awed by the grandness of the interior's 14 types of marble.

3. Built in 1927, the Hilton Checkers Los Angeles opened as The Mayflower Hotel with sculptures of the Mayflower and Santa Maria ships near the entrance. Its Spanish-style exterior is not the building's most defining characteristic; rather it is the narrowness of the hotel, which was built on a lot only 60 feet wide by 160 feet long. Its opening night party remains notorious in L. A. lore: The Mayflower brought in starlets dressed in capes who engaged in low conversation over sedate music until the band broke into lively Charleston music -- at which time the starlets ripped off their capes to reveal flapper costumes and began dancing.

4. The Los Angeles Central Library building has classic Art Deco-style architecture that draws the eye toward the sky with geometric shapes and a tall tower encasing a pyramid. Opened in 1926, the library extends for an entire city block. Suffering damage from the 1987 Whittier Narrows earthquake and two arson fires the previous year that ruined 30% of the library's collection, Central Library was in danger of being torn down by the city. A group of local citizens formed to stop destruction of the historic building, which needed $125 million in repairs. When a real estate developer said he would pay for the repairs as long as he could have air rights to the land, the city obliged, reopening the restored library in 1993.

5. Another Art Deco building, One Bunker Hill was constructed in 1931 to house the Southern California Edison Company that supplied electricity to all of Los Angeles. Resembling a cathedral, the building has 22 kinds of marble inside, along with coffered ceilings, burled walnut elevators, and many geometric shapes. The lobby features murals by artist Hugo Ballin, who at one time was also a major player in Hollywood's silent film industry. Now an office to Torrey Pines Bank and other businesses, the building was one of the first electrically heated and cooled buildings on the West Coast.

6. As the shortest railway in the world, Angels Flight Railway was built in 1900 as part of the fashionable Bunker Hill neighborhood. The funicular's two cars shuttled passengers up and down the steep hill between the top of Bunker Hill and the downtown area, for a total of 298 feet. Service was suspended in 2013, when a one-way ride still cost only 50 cents. The railway's board of directors is currently working to resolve regulatory issues to reopen Angels Flight.

7. Bunker Hill residents used Angels Flight Railway to get down the hill to Grand Central Market to shop for groceries. The open-air market was the city's first fireproofed and steel-reinforced structure, opened in 1917. Merchants and goods sold in the market have evolved with the times, from butchers and Jewish delis to today's ramen and ceviche. Renovated in the early 1990s as part of the revitalization of Grand Central Square, the market was purchased by urban developer Ira Yellin. His wife Adele has continued to oversee the project since Ira's death. The 30,000-square-foot marketplace is open daily and has nearly 100 vendors.

8. Named in honor of Army General John Pershing, Pershing Square is a popular public park originally settled as "La Plaza Abajo" in 1781 by Mexicans who established a pueblo on Olvera Street. The public square's close proximity to the river made it an ideal venue for community activities. Later, an underground parking structure was added in 1951, modeled after San Francisco's Union Square. Most recently renovated in 1994, the park that covers one square block is rumored to have a reconfiguration forthcoming.

The housing market continued to sputter in March as adverse weather, low supplies and higher costs discouraged home buyers.

Existing home sales declined 0.2% to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 4.59 million, the lowest level since July 2012, the National Association of Realtors said Tuesday. Home sales have fallen in seven of the past eight months. Still, the market was a bit firmer than expected. Economists' median estimate was for an annual sales rate of 4.57 million, according to Action Economics' survey.

A brutal winter continued to play a role in the weak showing. Although March brought milder temperatures and fewer storms, sales activity largely reflected closings on purchase agreements signed in January and February, says Jim O'Sullivan, chief U.S. economist of High Frequency Economics.

Other factors are also holding down sales. The average 30-year fixed mortgage rate is at 4.27%, low by historical standards but up from about 3.4% a year ago. And higher home prices and a tight supply of homes for sale discouraged some buyers, the NAR said.

There was a a 5.2-month supply of homes last month, up from a five-month supply in February, but six months is normal. Some of the tight inventory is welcome as the housing market recovers from the 2006 crash. Distressed homes, including foreclosures and short sales, accounted for 14% of sales in March, down from 21% a year ago.

But tight supplies limit selection and push up prices, crimping sales. The median price of a single-family home was $198,200 last month, up 7.4% from March 2013.

Another obstacle is that first-time buyers are making up an unusually low share of purchases -- 30%, down from 40% traditionally -- largely because of strict credit standards.

Some officials say brighter days are ahead. "There are indications that the stringent mortgage underwriting standards are beginning to ease a bit, particularly regarding credit score requirements," says NAR President Steve Brown.

Also, he says, brisk new-home construction will attract current homeowners, creating a bigger supply of existing homes for first- time buyers.

RealtyTrac reported 9.1 million houses, or 17% of all homes with mortgages, were "seriously underwater" in the first quarter. That means the total loan amount was at least 25% higher than the property's estimated market value.

This scar from the housing market crash is healing. The number of seriously underwater homes is nearly 2 million fewer than a year ago and 3.7 million fewer than in the second quarter of 2012, RealtyTrac said. In that quarter, 29% of all homes with mortgages were deeply underwater.

Underwater borrowers are a drag on the housing market and the economy. They don't move up to larger homes, and can't get home equity loans for expensive purchases.

"It's probably one of the things that is contributing to how lackluster housing has looked the last few months," says David Blitzer, head of Standard & Poor's Index Committee, producer of the Case-Shiller home price index.

The index shows prices have risen at an annual rate of 12% since bottoming in March 2012. But gains have slowed to single-digits in recent months.
