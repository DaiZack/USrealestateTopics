You've Got Wine! Georgia To Allow Direct Purchases
By Will Pinkston

The Wall Street Journal

Georgia wine connoisseurs soon can order New York Cabernet Sauvignon and California Chardonnay right to their doorsteps.

Under a new law that goes into effect Saturday, the state's residents can get home delivery of wine directly from wineries rather than having to purchase bottles at retail prices. Among other things, the new rule passed by the Legislature in February lets wine buffs place orders online and grants vineyards special shipping licenses.

Georgia is among 20 or so states where direct shipment of alcohol across state lines has been outlawed under Prohibition-era rules. In these states, wine typically has to pass through wholesalers and retailers, who mark up prices along the way.

Wholesalers' lobbyists often claim that wine and other alcohol shouldn't be shipped directly because it raises the risk of sales to underage drinkers; wineries dispute the argument, contending it's easy to confirm the identity and age of the recipient upon delivery.

Online clearinghouses have figured out ways to ship to some regulated states via wholesalers and retailers. But such roundabout tactics take time, cost more and still don't work in the most heavily restricted states -- including Alabama, Georgia and Tennessee in the Southeast.

Despite its freshly minted law permitting home delivery, Georgia isn't exactly an open wine market.

Rules allow individual wineries to ship up to 50 cases of any particular brand into the state each year (12 bottles to a case). And Georgia still hasn't approved beer and wine for home delivery by Internet grocers such as Webvan Group Inc., which pushed for it in the past legislative session.

The overall economy may be making a soft landing, but home prices in California are still climbing.

Last month, the median home price in California hit $203,000, up 1.5% from April and 9.7% from a year earlier, according to DataQuick Information Systems in La Jolla. The Bay Area -- Alameda, Contra Costa, Marin, Napa, San Francisco, San Mateo, Santa Clara, Solano and Sonoma counties -- posted a median price of $370,000, up 25% from a year earlier. The Board of Education recently approved a plan to build an $8 million, 43-unit apartment complex for teachers on district-owned land where a new elementary school, parking garage and child-care center are also slated for construction. The district plans to charge teachers below-market rents, ranging from $700 a month for a one-bedroom apartment to $1,300 a month for a three-bedroom unit. The board is also looking into building 50 to 100 housing units on other district-owned parcels.

"The salary level compared to housing costs, which are out of our control, make it very difficult for teachers to stay," says school-board President Mary Hernandez, noting that salaries start at $32,500 for new teachers and rise to $40,000 after five years. Housing assistance and higher salaries, she adds, "will help teachers to stay."

WASHINGTON -- The cost of buying a home became a little cheaper this week as the average rate on a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage dropped to 8.14%, according to a weekly survey released yesterday by Freddie Mac, the mortgage underwriter.

It was the fifth consecutive week that rates on long-term mortgages declined. Last week the rate on 30-year fixed mortgages averaged 8.22%. Last month, when fears of inflation were building and the economy appeared to be accelerating, long-term mortgage rates reached 8.64%, a five-year high. A year ago, the 30-year fixed-rate average was 7.63%.

The recent swing in mortgage rates reflects changes in investor perceptions about the economy. When it appeared that economic growth was too strong, mortgage rates began to rise. Recent signs that the economy could be cooling, which has eased fears of inflation, have helped pull rates back down again. Mortgage rates track yields on 10-year Treasury notes because mortgages tend to last about 10 years due to refinancing and relocations.

In the past several weeks alone, separate reports have pointed to slower job growth, consumer spending, and housing starts. Many economists are now predicting that the Federal Reserve Bank won't raise rates when it meets again next week.

Acxiom Corp.'s DataQuick unit, which monitors real-estate activity, estimates 49,200 homes were sold in May, up 12% from April and 3.5% from a year earlier.

The region's economic seas may be getting a bit choppy.

Mainly because of rising mortgage rates, residential building permits sagged to the lowest level since December 1997. The slowdown was most surprising in Georgia, the region's usual growth leader. In addition to a pullback in consumer spending, the state was the region's laggard in construction employment and average weekly hours. Among the causes, both in the Atlanta area: road-building projects stalled by air-pollution worries and layoffs by Lockheed Martin Corp. and Coca-Cola Co.

Other states fared better. Alabama was the only state to eke out a gain in residential construction. And North Carolina's manufacturers expanded their hours to fill orders interrupted by weather delays. Still, says Mr. Ratajczak, "It's too early to say if this is a trend, but there's no question April was not a good month."

Mr. Lynch and other community leaders have been locked in a fierce battle with Mr. Menino for control of the spoils of development proposed for 1,000 acres of waterfront land between downtown and South Boston.

WASHINGTON -- The Department of Housing and Urban Development unveiled a new antifraud initiative aimed at combating abusive lending tactics that take advantage of government-loan borrowers.

Under the program, the million-plus mortgage applicants whose loans are insured by HUD's Federal Housing Administration each year will be able to ensure that they are protected against abusive lending tactics by unscrupulous lenders. FHA-backed mortgage applicants will benefit from greater counseling in the loanwriting process and stricter policing of lenders, with government investigators pledging to pay closer attention to banks that charge steep fees or burdensome payments and penalties.

In addition, HUD officials said the initiative will monitor and track home sales to guard consumers against homes that have been "flipped," or bought on the cheap and sold at inflated prices, to protect FHA mortgage holders and ensure that they are guaranteed fair-market value for their new homes.

"The explosive growth in predatory lending has led to new scams and new schemes by which FHA homeowners are being defrauded and, in too many instances, forced into poverty," said HUD Secretary Andrew Cuomo.

Government officials said the new program, dubbed the Fraud Protection Plan, was established in response to a review of predatory lending abuses in the Baltimore area by a congressionally established HUD task force.

Sen. Barbara Mikulski (D., Md.), whose complaints about the state of housing in Baltimore triggered the special-task force investigation of allegations of flipping and other lender abuses, praised the new FHA initiative as "the first step toward protecting unsuspecting home buyers and repairing the damage that has been done to our community."

Under one of the most aggressive elements of the antifraud initiative, FHA investigators will work to identify mortgages that have been established based on fraudulent appraisals. If found, the FHA has pledged to force the given lender to write the mortgage down to a level consistent with true market value. If the lender hedges, the government will cancel the insurance on the loan, take possession of the deed to the house, and resell the property with FHA insurance to the family in the home for the estimated fair value of the dwelling.

Baltimore will be the first city to benefit from the new initiative, HUD said, but the program will soon be extended to other cities burdened by a troubled housing environment. Eventually, the initiative will be applied nationwide.

It's Hip to Flip Houses in a Market This Hot --- Builders, Neighbors Fume As Investors Buy Homes, Then Wait and Sell High
By Nancy D. Holt
Special to The Wall Street Journal
1259 words
10 May 2000
The Wall Street Journal
J

English
(Copyright (c) 2000, Dow Jones & Company, Inc.)

WHEN CONTINENTAL HOMES started selling new houses in the Denver suburb of Broomfield, Colo., people camped in line days ahead of time. The company sold 50 houses in six hours.

Michael Farrar, a Continental salesman, nabbed one of the first and best properties at Prairie Ridge at the Broadlands, agreeing to pay $225,200 for a ranch-style house on a plot backing the golf course. Warren Williams, a real-estate broker, signed a contract for a $215,415 home on another prime spot along the fairway. But Messrs. Farrar and Williams -- and about half the people who signed contracts that day in August 1998 -- had more in common than house-hunting success. None of them ever moved into the houses they bought.

Mr. Farrar's house was completed last June and he sold it the next month for $45,000 more than he paid. Mr. Williams didn't wait that long -- he sold his the day he closed, pocketing an instant $33,000 on the deal.

It's called flipping, and it's a practice that is becoming more common in red-hot residential markets such as Broomfield, where high-tech industries are attracting high-paying jobs. Investors are buying and rapidly reselling property in these areas, capitalizing on price appreciation seen during construction periods.

But Continental's Prairie Ridge at the Broadlands is an example of real-estate speculation run amok. In the end, investors purchased about 40% of the homes in the subdivision, according to on-site salespeople, and the homebuilder found itself competing with investors for sales in later phases.

A growing number of home builders are instituting policies designed to curb flipping -- or at least make it harder for investors to delay closings or walk away if the market sours. Continental, for example, has changed its rules to require investors to make nonrefundable, earnest-money deposits of 10% of the purchase price instead of paying a flat $2,000 or $3,000 depending on the model.

As flipping gains momentum, it is sparking a debate that pits capitalism against the American dream. On one side of the street are builders and residents who say flipping drives up prices and hinders community development.

"When home builders start selling to brokers, they don't create a neighborhood; they create a commodity," says Eddie Mechelay, an early buyer at Prairie Ridge who was disappointed to find himself living amid so many "For Rent" and "For Sale" signs.

On the other side of the debate are investors who say the practice has no bearing on prices and preserves inventory for buyers who can't wait six to nine months for a new home. "We're providing a service" by creating supply in sold-out neighborhoods, says Sam Cardenas, a broker associate at Maes & Co. Realtors Inc. in Denver.

Flipping tends to occur in areas with big employment gains and residential growth limits. The locales have varied through the years, from Boston to San Diego -- with investors recently returning to California -- but the run-up in prices remains the same, says Kenneth Agid, a real-estate consultant in Newport Beach, Calif. He says speculation artificially boosts demand, fueling a buying frenzy and an inflationary spiral. "Someone who could have afforded a house at a reasonable price is precluded from that opportunity, but we never hear their stories," says Mr. Agid.

In Broomfield, investors say soaring prices simply reflect consumer demand in a city that has attracted thousands of jobs and limited housing. Last year, businesses such as Sun Microsystems Inc., of Palo Alto, Calif., and Level 3 Communications Inc., based in Broomfield, ushered in 2,500 employees, while the city instituted a growth cap that allows about 600 single-family home permits annually. Neighboring cities have limited permits, as well, and more jobs are coming.

The average price of a single-family home in the Broomfield area jumped 19% in 1999 to $200,200, compared with 12% in the Denver metro area, according to Genesis Group Inc. in Englewood, Colo. But for potential buyers, the bigger problem was availability. New homes under $250,000 meant lists or long nights in a tent, driving prospects to outlying municipalities.

So when Continental promised homes from $160,000 to $230,000 in the Broadlands, a planned golf-course community with home prices into the millions, buyers took notice. But much of the demand was created by real-estate brokers and investors. Mr. Mechelay, who camped out for three nights to buy a lot, says that fewer than half of the 30-odd buyers lined up on the first day were buying homes for themselves. And many investors bought more than one house.

Mr. Farrar, the salesman, who is no longer with Continental, signed up for two homes that day and says it was a "win-win for everyone. It created buying momentum. People would come in and see the amount of activity, and the fear of loss kicked in."

Nonetheless, Continental soon found itself competing against the investors, says Andrea Lard, a latecomer to the sales team who ended up with the last lots, nearly all of the paperwork and more than her share of headaches. Investors scrambling for financing and buyers tried to delay closings; some pushed to make last-minute design changes to suit prospects; and some offered her bribes to entice her to help market their properties. "I just told people it would be a conflict of interest," says Ms. Lard, who recently resigned. Continental, a division of home builder D.R. Horton Inc., based in Dallas, declined to comment.

With speculation, some builders have changed their methods, instituting lotteries, increasing deposits or prohibiting investor sales outright. Littleton-based Village Homes of Colorado Inc., for example, this year started holding all buyers to just one home. "By limiting opportunities for investors, we're reserving more opportunities for families," says Cheryl Schuette, vice president of sales and marketing at Village Homes. "There are enough people who want new homes for themselves."

But other builders say their experiences have been positive. Investors took 25 of the last 40 lots in Broomfield offered by Centex Homes, a division of Centex Corp. in Dallas, without any delays or impact on the builder's sales, says Mark Krivel, division president. "It may be causing some inflationary pressure on prices, as they're trying to maximize revenues," he adds. "But they're taking a risk."

Real-estate speculators choose midsize models and start marketing before completion. As few builders allow buyers to assign contracts, investors usually close on their properties, using cash, conventional loans, lease-option purchases or lines of credit.

Flipping tends to attract real-estate brokers because they can sell the properties themselves, saving on commissions. In addition, brokers get a fee of 3% of the base price from the builder when they bring in a buyer, even if they are the buyer.

As to whether the practice is unfair to consumers, investors are quick to point out that business isn't always fair, or benevolent. "It's like saying that Microsoft should set aside a bunch of stock for people who really want to keep it forever," says Mr. Williams, a broker at Re/Max Alliance, a franchise of Re/Max International Inc. in Denver. "The argument loses credibility when you take out the emotion."
