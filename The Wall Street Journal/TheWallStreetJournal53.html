U.S. Data Fuel Recovery Hopes --- Business Orders, Housing, Confidence Rise, as Do Signals of Spring Rebound --- But Nondefense Investment Remains Below Pre-Sept. 11 Pace
By Robert Gavin
Staff Reporter
917 words
31 December 2001
The Asian Wall Street Journal
AWSJ
2
English
(Copyright (c) 2001, Dow Jones & Company, Inc.)

Business investment, housing and consumer confidence in the U.S. all showed surprising and widespread improvement, adding to the evidence an end to the recession could be in sight.

In a wave of economic indicators released Friday, orders for new capital equipment rose in November after a similar gain in October -- a hopeful sign since slumping business spending has been a dominant characteristic of the current recession. Other positives included a big jump in consumer confidence in December, stronger sales of both new and existing homes in November, and a third straight week of relatively slow growth in unemployment insurance claims.

"What we're beginning to see more and more is that the sharpest contractions are over, and we're starting to build for an economic recovery in the spring," said Steven A. Wood, chief economist for FinancialOxygen Inc., a financial services firm based in Walnut Creek, California.

Orders for durable goods, which are designed to last three or more years, fell 4.8% in November, the Commerce Department said, after surging a record 12.8% in October. But that swing was dominated by defense orders. In October, early bookings associated with the $200 billion Joint Strike Fighter contract to Lockheed Martin Corp., helped send defense orders up 218% in October. In November, they fell back to Earth, plummeting 70%. Excluding the volatile defense sector, November orders were up for the second consecutive month, rising 2.7% on top of October's 4.8% gain.

Orders for nondefense capital goods, a crucial indicator of business investment, rose 4.8% in November, after increasing 5.9% in October. Technology orders were up 2%; motor vehicles rose 4.5%; electrical equipment increased 2.6%; and machinery gained 2.4%. Additionally, inventories fell 1.1% in November, leading Thomas J. Duesterberg, president and chief executive officer of the Arlington, Virginia-based Manufacturers Alliance, to predict a recovery by spring. "Inventories have been pared back so much that production is likely to pick up even more as we enter the new year," he said.

Many analysts, however, noted that civilian durable-goods orders remained below the pre-Sept. 11 pace, and said a rebound may not be imminent. "It's still too early to know whether the manufacturing sector is bouncing back for real," Mark Vitner, economist for Wachovia Securities, said in a report.

Nonetheless, economists noted other positive signs. The Conference Board's index of consumer confidence jumped to 93.7 this month from 84.9 in November, putting it near pre-Sept. 11 levels. The increase was in line with results of the University of Michigan's index of consumer sentiment, which jumped to 88.8 this month from 83.9 in November. The Conference Board said the December rise was driven by a sharp jump in the expectations index, which measures consumers' feelings about the future.

The improvement in consumer sentiment could be the product of a stabilizing job market. Initial claims for unemployment benefits rose by 7,000 last week to 392,000, the Labor Department said. It was the third consecutive week that new jobless claims remained below 400,000, the level above which some analysts say overall employment starts to contract, and significantly below the 535,000 new claims reported the last week of September. The four-week moving average, a less volatile measure, continued to drop, to 413,000, from 439,000 the week before -- the lowest level since Sept. 15.

Meanwhile, the housing market continues to defy the recession, helped by low interest rates and mild weather in much of the U.S. The average fixed rate for a 30-year mortgage was 6.66% in November, more than a point lower than the average rate a year earlier. The National Association of Realtors reported Friday that existing-home sales could possibly break the record of 5.21 million units set in 1999. November sales matched that pace, the association said, up 0.6% from a 5.18 million pace in October. New-home sales rose jumped to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 934,000 units, up 6.4% from 878,000 in October, the Commerce Department reported.

However, the construction industry continues to lose some momentum, as construction starts fell 3% in November, according to the latest report from F.W. Dodge. The value of new-construction contracts fell to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of $478.2 billion, owing to a decline in housing and public-works projects, said F.W. Dodge, a building-research division of publisher McGraw-Hill Cos. Nonresidential buildings showed improvement for the third month in a row, the report said.

"The November figures point toward a construction industry that continues to settle back gradually from its heightened performance at the outset of 2001," said Robert A. Murray, vice president of economic affairs for F.W. Dodge.

The Dodge Index, a measure of national construction value that uses 1996 as a base year of 100, was at 144 for November, down from 148 in October. The index began the year at 153 but weakened during the summer, the report said.

By Ray A. Smith
Staff Reporter of The Wall Street Journal

The Wall Street Journal

(Copyright (c) 2001, Dow Jones & Company, Inc.)

Construction starts fell 3% in November, indicating the industry continues to lose some momentum.

The value of new-construction contracts fell to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of $478.2 billion, due to a decline in housing and public-works projects, according to the latest report from F.W. Dodge, a building-research division of publisher McGraw-Hill Cos. Nonresidential buildings showed improvement for the third month in a row, the report said.

"The November figures point toward a construction industry that continues to settle back gradually from its heightened performance at the outset of 2001," said Robert A. Murray, vice president of economic affairs for F.W. Dodge. "It's important to note that the loss of momentum for total construction has been very mild, as a result of an offsetting pattern by the industry's major sectors."

When commercial building declined during the first half of 2001, Mr. Murray said, some of the shortfall was picked up by high-level activity for housing, public works and power plants. Now, he said, housing and public works appear to be slipping back, but the institutional building sector, namely school construction, continues to be strong.

The Dodge Index, a measure of national construction value that uses 1996 as a base year of 100, was at 144 for November, down from 148 in October. The index began the year at 153 but weakened during the summer, the report said.

Residential building in November dropped 5% to $202.6 billion compared with the prior month. Single-family housing fell 6% in dollar volume, while multifamily housing remained steady. "For most of 2001, single-family housing has been able to hold up quite well, as low mortgage rates have countered the negatives of weak consumer confidence and rising layoffs," Mr. Murray said. "However, it now appears that these negatives are beginning to have a greater dampening effect, which means that single-family housing will likely see a continued retreat in the next few months."

Nonbuilding construction fell 18% to $91.1 billion. A 30% decline was reported for public works such as highways, bridges, sewers and water-supply systems, but Mr. Murray pointed out that the sector is subject to occasional volatility month by month. He said November should be viewed as a "correction" that brings public works more in line with the 6% increase reported by the sector for the first 11 months of 2001.

Nonresidential building grew by 10% to $184.5 billion. The institutional side of the nonresidential market -- schools, courthouses, detention facilities, health-care facilities and amusement-related projects -- was strong, the report said. On the commercial side, warehouse construction fell 8% and hotel construction fell 36%. However, construction of stores and shopping centers rose 9%, while office construction rose 26%.

The report showed that single-family housing starts rose 3.2% in November, while housing starts on multifamily units and apartment buildings surged 30%.

Separately, Redbook Research's latest indicator of U.S. retail sales showed sales fell 4.6% in the first two retailing weeks of December from the same period in November. The report also showed that seasonally adjusted sales fell 1.1% from the year-earlier period.

Redbook said that on an unadjusted basis, sales in the week ended Dec. 15 fell 1.2% from the year-earlier week. Redbook added that performance slowed a bit in the second week of December, although most retailers' sales went according to plan. The report said sales growth is expected to be strongest this week and next, as consumers continue to delay purchases until right before Christmas.

But after nearly a decade of upward momentum, housing prices in selected areas seem to have lost a bit of their irrational exuberance. In wealthy places, such as parts of Seattle, New York and Silicon Valley, this spring's stock market jitters have put the brakes on runaway price appreciation. In other, less affluent spots, interest rate run-ups have priced some home buyers out of the market. And in fast-growing sectors of cities such as San Diego and Washington, D.C., there are now more homes on the market, helping curb price growth.

We went looking around the country for areas that are now seeing falling prices or where prices are showing signs of peaking or even passing their peak. What we found was some cooling even in places where the market is still sizzling. While loft prices in the trendy SoHo neighborhood of Manhattan rose an annualized 42% in the first quarter of this year, for example, local real-estate brokers report a slight downturn in neighboring TriBeCa: Open-house traffic is off by half and bidding wars are starting to abate. In other areas, such as Boulder, Colo., and parts of Houston, sellers are offering modest but until recently unheard of incentives, from free appliances to help with financing.

The Seattle situation is similarly mixed. Median prices rose 8.3% to $226,100 for the first quarter, compared with a year ago. But real-estate brokers say much of the growth is being driven by the city's traffic problems. Places in town or near high-tech centers east of the city are in such demand that desperate buyers go as far as writing letters praising the seller's housekeeping, or enclosing pictures of their kids to try to clinch a deal, broker and appraiser Ed Murphy says.

But further out in Snohomish County, north of the city, the sellers are the ones getting desperate. Brian and Sharon Ward have been trying to sell their eight-year-old custom-built home for more than two years, lowering their initial $799,000 asking price to $779,900. Although the lakefront home has four marble fireplaces, a seven-foot Italian crystal chandelier and a mahogany deck, buyers have shown little interest. "On the East side, people are paying $1.5 million for tear-downs," says Mr. Ward, who owns a heating-and-air-conditioning company. "I can't even get people out to see this property."

To be sure, home sales typically taper off in the summer. But real-estate agents say some of what's happening this year hasn't been seen since the real-estate slowdown of the early '90s and can't simply be called a summer slump.

In Houston, for example, Paul and Julie Musser just snagged a four-bedroom white colonial in upper-middle-class Jersey Village. They got the house, which has a pool and gazebo, for $254,000 -- about $10,000 less than the asking price -- and got $1,000 at closing to spend on remodeling. That's hardly a steal, of course, but it's a far cry from the frenzy of recent years.

"We feel like we were at the right place at the right time," says Mrs. Musser, who closed on the house last month. Overall, median prices in Houston rose a respectable 7.5% to $105,800 for the first quarter, but prices have recently weakened in upscale areas such as Jersey Village, where plenty of homes are for sale.

Indeed, the National Association of Realtors' recent survey of 128 major metropolitan areas showed that in the first quarter, median home prices fell in 37 cities -- including South Bend, Ind., Shreveport, La., and Portland, Maine -- compared with a year earlier. Only 21 cities -- among them, Omaha, Neb., Baltimore and San Diego -- showed the double-digit increases. By contrast, in the first quarter of 1998, 23 cities showed double-digit jumps, while median prices fell in only five cities. "It's starting to seep into the public consciousness that things are slowing down," says Fred Flick, the trade group's vice president of economic research. "Buyers are becoming more cautious."

Karl Case, an economist with a Cambridge, Mass., research firm that compiles quarterly home-price statistics, adds that, while home prices overall are likely to keep going up, many areas are nearing or past their peak. He says the number of second-quarter sales of new and existing homes has declined, a sign the economy may be slowing despite continued strength in job growth. "People have been buying on the expectation that the economy doesn't have to go down, but you can't repeal the business cycle," he says. "I think people ought to worry."

In some places, they already do. In Biloxi, Miss., for instance, median home prices plummeted 25.4% for the first quarter, to $65,500 -- the worst drop in the country, according to the National Association of Realtors. Local broker Ray Jones blames the increase in interest rates this spring. Although mortgage rates have gone down, they are still around 8%, about 1.5 percentage points higher than they were 21 months ago. "We have very few buyers here who can afford to pay cash for homes, so when rates went up, people simply quit buying," he says.

In wealthier regions, such as the San Francisco Bay area, supply is driving prices. The city has had a chronic shortage of homes for sale, which has pushed prices up relentlessly -- during the first quarter alone, they rose a blistering 25% from a year earlier, to $418,600, the highest increase in the nation. But now increased competition is forcing sellers to curb some of their more outlandish demands.

In the hot $5 million-and-up range, real-estate agent Carol MacCorkle says, sellers are no longer insisting buyers accept their homes "as is," even if there is a major problem such as termite damage. That is a big change from January, when hardly any homes in that range were available in Silicon Valley, and buyers were sweetening deals by paying in cash or offering to close in as few as five days.

Today, market jitters are taking a toll. "We're Nasdaq-sensitive," says Ms. MacCorkle, who has seen fewer bidding wars since the spring. Appreciation is slowing down a bit too: In wealthy Atherton, Calif., prices rose 40% in the first quarter, she says, but now are rising at a pace of about 25% to 30%. That's because there are now five times as many upper-end homes on the market than there were in January, when only six homes were listed.

In more stable markets, there are also indications that things may be getting a little better for buyers. About a month ago, Stu and Julie Damon sold their colonial home in a chic Washington, D.C., suburb for $538,000 -- $13,000 more than their asking price -- in a bidding war that erupted almost immediately after they listed it. So when they started looking in an equally fashionable neighborhood in Richmond, Va., about 60 miles away, they figured they would have to fight to get the home they wanted.

Not so. In less than a month, the market had settled just enough so that they paid only the asking price -- $525,000 -- for their home, and they got the seller to shell out $2,500 in repairs. "I think we sold at the peak," Mrs. Damon. "Now prices seem like they're beginning to soften."

When area prices soften, builders are loath to lower home prices in communities they have already started work on, because they don't want to anger buyers who purchased early. Instead, they offer incentives to buy, such as free sunrooms or finished basements, or help with closing costs. Although it is still too early for builders to give away such expensive goodies, some are starting to offer modest lures to skittish buyers -- even in such hot markets as Denver, where median prices rose nearly 13% during the first quarter from the year-earlier period.

Denver marketing consultant Robert August, who represents 50 local builders, says that in the past few weeks, some of his clients have become so nervous about the economy that they are giving away free appliances, offering to help pay mortgage points, and reducing commissions for on-site sales agents. Buyers, for their part, are becoming more demanding. Mr. August says one of his clients recently got a contract where the buyer wanted full landscaping, free air conditioning and a gas grill. "Six months ago, no one would have dared to ask for that."

Oddly enough, second homes seem to be impervious to any weakening in home prices. Rick Moeser, who heads the Southern regional office for Sotheby's International Realty, says there has been "no slippage at all in prices" for his high-end division, in which sales average $1.7 million. In fact, young Wall Street turks paying more than $1 million for two-bedroom Manhattan condominiums tend to view similarly priced Southeastern oceanfront estates in resort areas as bargains, he says. "People are paying the asking price, no questions asked."

In Southampton, N.Y., another summer mecca for Manhattanites, real-estate broker Jack Hangen says prices have risen at a rate of about 20% for the past year and a half and show no signs of backsliding. He thinks the reason prices haven't fallen off -- even amid a wobbly stock market -- is that the East Coast financiers who are snapping up the properties aren't arrivistes relying on "dot-com money."

"We're seeing people who struck it rich a decade ago when they were in their 30s," Mr. Hangen says. "Now they're in their 40s and have families; they've become more conservative in their investments. And they're buying these vacation houses with their bonuses."
