WASHINGTON -- A barrage of sunny economic data poured in Wednesday, led by an upbeat regional report from the Federal Reserve's latest anecdotal survey of the economy.

The Federal Reserve's survey of business activity found that economic conditions were improving across the country and appear to be "reasonably broadly based," with most areas noting growth in a number of industries.

Eleven of the 12 regional Fed districts reported faster growth, except the Atlanta district, which said the pace of economic growth was "stable" in October and early November. "Labor markets across the nation generally improved or remained stable, with several districts noting a slowing in layoffs," the Fed said. Temporary workers are in increasing demand in the Boston, Minneapolis, Chicago and Richmond, Va., districts, the Fed said. But several districts reported that companies are waiting for sustained increases in orders before hiring permanent workers.

Also Wednesday, there were two upbeat reports on the U.S. manufacturing sector, which was the hardest-hit part of the economy during the 2001 recession. Orders for big-ticket items surged in October and a regional manufacturing index soared to a nine-year high in November. "The factory sector is on fire," said Stephen Stanley, senior market economist at RBS Greenwich Capital.

The Fed survey, prepared for use at the Dec. 9 Federal Open Market Committee meeting, also provided some positive news about the retail sector, reporting that spending was up on a year-over-year basis, with retailers "generally positive" about holiday sales.

But the report also suggested that the breakneck speed of spending that caused economic growth to surge during the summer months is easing. The Department of Commerce reported this week that gross domestic product, the value of the nation's output adjusted for inflation, grew at an annual rate of 8.2% in the July-to-September quarter. But that pace of growth is unlikely to be repeated.

The Fed survey found that auto sales, for example, were down in many regions, as auto makers cut back on generous factory incentives. The St. Louis district said incentives may have led many households to buy cars in the summer, rather than waiting until fall. One retailer in the Richmond district said sales patterns show consumers were living "paycheck to paycheck," now that the effects of this summer's tax refunds were lessening.

Other economic data released Wednesday also indicated a moderation in consumer spending. The Commerce Department reported that while personal income rose 0.4% in October -- the biggest one-month gain since June -- personal consumption was unchanged last monsth after sinking 0.3% the month before. Spending on durable goods -- big-ticket items such as cars and appliances -- fell 2.3% last month, and spending on nondurable goods edged lower. But household spending on services rose 0.5%.

Most other economic news released Wednesday was upbeat:

-- The Purchasing Management Association of Chicago's business-activity index rose to 64.1 for November from 55 for the previous month. This was its seventh straight monthly gain and the highest level since October 1994. The Chicago survey is watched closely for clues to the index of the Institute for Supply Management, which will be released Monday. A reading above 50 indicates expansion in the manufacturing sector, while a reading below 50 indicates a contraction.

The Chicago report was well above market expectations and showed impressive gains in new orders and production. Its employment component retreated below 50, suggesting weaker conditions, but other job market data were more favorable.

-- Durable-goods orders rose 3.3% to $184.5 billion last month, the biggest increase in 15 months, the Commerce Department said. Also, September orders were revised upward to show a 2.1% gain, first reported as a 1.1% increase. The gains outpaced forecasts of a 0.6% rise.

-- New home sales dropped 3.5% in October, according to the Commerce Department. But the pace of home sales stayed strong at a 1.1 million annual rate.

-- The Labor Department said new claims for jobless benefits fell for the eighth straight week, reaching a 34-month low. Initial claims fell by 11,000 to 351,000 in the week ended Nov. 22, marking the lowest level since the week President Bush was inaugurated. Analysts said the drop supported evidence that the economy is finally starting to generate jobs.

-- U.S. consumer sentiment rose to its highest level since May 2002, according the University of Michigan's survey on consumer mood. The survey's index stood at 93.7 at the end of November, compared with 93.5 in the preliminary November estimate and 89.6 in October. "Households in general are feeling better about their economic lots in life because the employment situation is improving and the stock market is improving," said Paul Kasriel, director of economic research at Northern Trust Securities, Chicago.

WASHINGTON -- Sunny U.S. economic data poured in Wednesday, led by manufacturing gains and job-market improvements, while the U.S. Federal Reserve's latest survey showed the American economy continued to steam ahead in October and early November.

Orders for big-ticket items surged in October, and a regional-manufacturing index soared to a nine-year high. At the same time, new claims for unemployment benefits continued to fall in recent weeks, personal income rose, and consumer confidence improved.

A 3.5% drop in new-home sales was the only news that was less than rosy. But the pace of home sales stayed strong at an annual rate of 1.105 million, according to the U.S. Commerce Department's report, and the decline was only a little bigger than the market expected.

"These numbers are really quite strong," said Stephen Stanley, senior market economist at RBS Greenwich Capital. "The factory sector is on fire."

Durable-goods orders rose 3.3% to $184.5 billion (156.55 billion euros) last month, the biggest rise in 15 months, the U.S. Commerce Department said. September orders were revised upward to show a 2.1% gain, first reported as a 1.1% rise. The gain outpaced forecasts of a 0.6% rise, and orders climbed in almost all categories except autos.

Separately, the Purchasing Management Association of Chicago's business-activity index rose to 64.1 in November from 55 the previous month. This was its seventh-straight monthly gain and the highest level since October 1994. The Chicago survey is watched closely for clues on the index of the Institute for Supply Management, which will be released Monday. A reading above 50 indicates expansion in the manufacturing sector, and a reading below 50 indicates contraction. The Chicago report showed impressive gains in new orders and production. Its employment component fell below 50, but other job-market data were more favorable.

The U.S. Labor Department said new claims for jobless benefits fell for the eighth-straight week, setting a 34-month low. Initial claims for the week ended Nov. 22 fell by 11,000 to 351,000, marking the lowest level since the week U.S. President George W. Bush was inaugurated.

Analysts said the drop supported evidence that the economy is starting to generate jobs after a long dry spell. "That's a very nice decline. It suggests some of the unemployed are starting to make their way back to the work force," said economist Mickey Levy of Banc of America Securities.

Indeed, the U.S. Fed said in its latest beige-book report that the labor market showed signs of stabilizing. "Labor markets across the nation generally improved or remained stable, with several districts noting a slowing in layoffs," the Fed said. The Boston, Minneapolis, Chicago and Richmond, Virginia, districts noted greater demand for temporary workers. Firms are waiting for sustained increases in orders before hiring permanent workers, several districts said.

"Reports received from the district banks suggest that the economy continued to expand in October and early November," the Fed said. Economic improvements in the 12 districts appear to be "reasonably broadly based," with most noting growth in a number of industries, it said. Eleven of the 12 regional Fed districts reported faster growth, except the Atlanta district, which said the pace of economic growth was "stable" in October and early November.

The beige book is a summary of economic activity prepared for use at the U.S. central bank's next Federal Open Market Committee meeting Dec. 9. The most recent report reflects information gathered before Nov. 17.

The report follows news earlier this week that the economy grew at an eye-popping 8.2% pace in the third quarter, propelled by faster consumer spending.

Household incomes are picking up. Consumers earned more in October but didn't change their spending habits, the U.S. Commerce Department said. Personal income rose 0.4% in October after climbing 0.3% in September.

Personal consumption was unchanged in October after sinking 0.3% the previous month. Also, U.S. consumer sentiment in November rose to its highest level since May 2002, according to the University of Michigan's survey on consumer mood.

Company Profits Soared by 30% In 3rd Quarter --- Biggest Gain in Two Decades Came as GDP Is Revised Up; Red-Hot Pace Seen Cooling

The economic recovery has picked up momentum, boosting the bottom lines of a wide range of U.S. corporations.

The Commerce Department reported that profits at American companies rose 30% in the third quarter, compared with a year earlier. That was the largest year-over-year growth in profits in 19 years and was enough to lift the annual pace of profits above $1 trillion for the first time in history.

The corporate profit figures were part of a broader report on economic output, or gross domestic product. The government revised up its estimate of the annual rate of third-quarter GDP to 8.2% from the previously reported annual pace of 7.2%. The revision in GDP -- the total value of the nation's output of goods and services -- occurred largely because companies didn't reduce inventories as aggressively as previously thought in the face of booming consumer demand. Less inventory reduction meant companies had to produce more goods to keep up with demand.

For many companies, the combination of faster economic growth (which spurs revenue gains) and rapid productivity improvement (which helps contain costs) translates to stronger profits. Better profits, in turn, are expected to give business executives added confidence to hire more workers and invest more aggressively in new projects.

"This is making us more confident that this [recovery] is sustainable," said James Glassman, an economist with J.P. Morgan Chase &Co. "Hiring is already under way and my guess is it is going to accelerate."

Underscoring that point, the Conference Board said its index of consumer confidence rose to 91.7 in November from 81.7 in October, as fewer of the 5,000 people contacted in the group's monthly mail survey said jobs were hard to get.

Moreover, in the revision of its GDP estimate, the Commerce Department said business investment in equipment and software grew at an 18% annual rate during the third quarter, faster than the 15% capital-spending rate originally estimated.

The numbers are a positive development for President Bush's re-election drive. By mid-morning, Bush campaign strategist Matthew Dowd had compiled a graph comparing consumer-confidence trends during the Bush administration with parallel periods in the Nixon, Reagan and first Bush presidencies. "We're about where Reagan and Nixon were," he said, referring to two Republicans who won re-election in landslides. A recent Wall Street Journal/NBC poll found that, for the first time this year, more voters approved of Mr. Bush's handling of the economy than disapproved.

Democrats, in turn, are concentrating on Mr. Bush's potential economic soft spots, citing such issues as aid to minority businesses, improving public schools and shortcomings in early childhood education.

The stock market, which had anticipated the strong economic news, managed only modest gains yesterday.

Few economists, however, believe the current level of economic activity is sustainable. Indeed, the latest survey of economic forecasters by WSJ.com calls for GDP to grow at a rate of about 4% in the October-December period. Why? Last summer's boom in mortgage refinancing to take advantage of low interest rates and President Bush's tax cuts and child-tax credits will fade in the months ahead.

While many economists expect the economy to cool from its red-hot pace, the chances of the recovery fizzling, as it did in 2002, seem to be diminishing. "I'm going to hire some people because I think [a recovery] is here now," said Bill Zadrozny, chief executive of the North American arm of Siemens Financial Services, the financing unit of Siemens AG of Germany. The company, which finances capital-equipment purchases for Siemens' customers and other companies, is expecting its North American asset base to grow to $3.6 billion in the current fiscal year, which began Oct. 1, from $3.2 billion.

The government estimated that corporate profits rose to an annual rate of $1.003 trillion in the third quarter from $771 billion in the third quarter of 2002 and $897 billion in the second quarter of 2003, on a seasonally adjusted basis. The report was the first peek at economywide profits for the third quarter.

The government's initial estimate of gross domestic product, which was made last month, included estimates of overall economic activity, but not profits. Those figures are the government's broadest measure of profits for the whole U.S. economy and include listed and nonlisted companies. It makes adjustments for changes in the value of inventories and for depreciation and changes in the value of capital equipment. While some other measures of profitability have been skewed by changes in the tax law enacted earlier this year, Kenneth Petrick, a Commerce Department statistician, said this broad measure isn't affected and offers the best picture of profit trends.

Other measures of corporate profitability have turned decisively higher during the past six months. Among 1,489 companies tracked by Dow Jones &Co. in the US Total Market Index, net income rose 70% in the third quarter from a year earlier, to $133 billion. The figures are those actually reported by listed companies under generally accepted accounting principles, without the many special adjustments that Wall Street analysts often make to the numbers. The figures also aren't recorded at an annual rate, as are the Commerce Department figures.

Energy and financial companies were big winners in the third quarter. Exxon Mobil Corp., for instance, said its net income increased 38% to $3.65 billion, thanks to higher oil and natural-gas prices. Chevron Texaco Corp. swung to a profit of $1.975 billion from a loss of $904 million. Meanwhile, JP Morgan Chase &Co. said profit rose to $1.63 billion from $40 million, due to a significant reduction in bad loans to the corporate sector. Citigroup Inc. said profit rose 20% to $4.69 billion.

Some sectors, such as steel, tobacco and pharmaceuticals deteriorated, but overall, the gains were broad-based. Among 87 industries tracked by Dow Jones, 66 saw profits improving. Of the 235 technology companies such as Dell Inc. and Intel Corp. included in the total, the bottom line swung to a collective profit of $8.9 billion from a collective loss of $7 billion. Included among them was a gain for Internet service companies, such as Yahoo Inc. Soft-drink makers, including Coca Cola Co. and Pepsico Inc., saw a 14% increase in reported net income. Industrial companies -- ranging from 3M Co. to Deere &Co. to Solectron Corp. -- collectively saw a 70% improvement after a miserable 2002. Cyclical consumer companies -- from General Motors Corp. to Northwest Airlines to RadioShack Corp. -- altogether reported an 87% improvement.

"No matter how you slice it, this was a terrific quarter," said Chuck Hill, director of research at Boston-based Thomson First Call, which tracks corporate earnings.

Profits are expected to remain strong at least through the end of the year. Mr. Hill projects fourth-quarter profits for companies that make up the S&P 500 index to be up 25% from a year earlier, after a 22% increase in the third quarter. That number is after so-called extraordinary items, such as one-time restructuring charges, are stripped out. During the first half of next year, such S&P 500 operating profits are expected to grow by 12% from a year earlier.

Two other factors might slow the pace of overall economic growth in the months ahead. First, because companies didn't reduce inventories as much as previously thought during the third quarter, there is a chance they won't be as aggressive as some economists expect them to be in building up new inventories in the fourth quarter. Second, it is possible that imports could surge again, after growing at a relatively slow pace of 1.5% in the third quarter. Increasing imports detract from domestic output.

Separately, the National Association of Realtors said sales of existing homes softened for the first time in five months in October but remained at elevated levels. Sales fell 4.9% from the previous month's record sales pace, to an annual rate of 6.35 million units.

It's hammer time in housing.

Defying expectations of a slowdown, home builders quickened the pace of new construction in October. Housing starts rose 2.9% from the month before to a seasonally adjusted annual rate of 1.96 million units, the highest rate of activity since January 1986, the Commerce Department said.

The construction increase provides some assurance overall economic activity won't fizzle in the fourth quarter after a booming third quarter. The Commerce Department also revised up its estimates of housing starts in September and August.

"For the fourth quarter we expected that housing would not be adding to growth in [economic output], but now it looks like it almost certainly will," said Michael Carliner, economist with the National Association of Home Builders. The association released a survey Tuesday that showed confidence among builders fell slightly in November after reaching its highest point in four years in October.

The government estimates the overall economy grew at a 7.2% annual rate during the third quarter, though that number is expected to be revised up when it is updated Tuesday. Economists are projecting a 4% annualized rate of growth in the fourth quarter. Home building accounts for a little more than 4% of all economic output, or gross domestic product.

Many economists doubt the recent rate of new-home construction is sustainable, and some believe the housing market could be at the center of the next financial bubble for the nation. Yet the latest report suggested home construction should continue to rise at least for the next few months. The October report showed that building permits, an indicator of future building activity, rose 5.2% to a 1.97 million annual rate. "Financing rates remain quite low from a longer-run perspective. And an expected pickup in income growth should provide an offset to help keep housing affordability near its record highs. Also, inventories of unsold homes remain quite depressed," David Greenlaw, an economist with Morgan Stanley, said in a report to clients.

Housing starts across the nation were mixed. New activity rose 17.7% in the West. Steven Berman, a Commerce Department statistician, said there was no evidence wildfires in California during the month had a significant impact on the data. Typically, it takes many months before disasters such as wildfires or hurricanes lead to new building activity. Starts fell in the Northeast by 18%. They declined in the Midwest by 8% while increasing in the fast-growing South by 4.9%.

Separately, the Mortgage Bankers Association said its index measuring applications for mortgages to purchase new homes increased 13.5% in the week ended Nov. 14 from the previous week to 425.9. That was up 23% from a year earlier.

The association said mortgage borrowers increasingly favor adjustable-rate mortgages over fixed-rate mortgages. Adjustable-rate mortgages have financing rates that can be changed based on movements in shorter-term interest rates. They now carry lower interest costs than fixed-rate mortgages, which are fixed over long periods of time. In mid-November, adjustable-rate mortgages accounted for 27.5% of mortgage applications, the highest share in more than three years.

HIGHER MORTGAGE rates don't just take the fun out of refinancing. They could also reduce home prices in some parts of the country.

As interest rates rise from the summer's lows, that is one of the bigger risks in the economy for Americans whose best-performing asset over the past few years has been their home. But it would be a mixed blessing for buyers already priced out of many markets. They might pay less for the house but would face higher borrowing costs. A 25-city analysis by Fidelity National Financial Inc., a real-estate services provider, shows that rising rates will have a sharply different impact on various regions of the country. The study, conducted at the request of The Wall Street Journal, found that many of the markets that have seen the sharpest appreciation now stand to be the hardest hit.

For example, a median-priced home in Boston, currently worth $407,000, would see its price tumble by $59,000 by 2006, if the rate on a 30-year fixed-rate mortgage climbed nearly two percentage points to 8% next year and stayed there, according to the Fidelity National study. Other vulnerable markets include several California cities, Seattle and Washington, D.C.

Fidelity's analysis suggests that rising rates will cause the most pain in markets on the East and West coasts where it's hard to build new homes to meet rising demand. "Because people know there's a limited supply of homes for sale . . . you get almost a frenzy," says Fidelity chief valuation officer Michael Sklarz.

Rising rates would have a much more muted effect in less frothy markets like Pittsburgh, where the median home sells for just $108,000. If the rate on a 30-year mortgage rose to 8%, that home would still rise by $6,000 in price. The Fidelity analysis looked at how rising rates would affect home prices in the 25 largest metropolitan areas.

Interest rates play a far more important role in housing than in any other consumer market. Because homes cost so much and because they're financed over such a long period of time, even small changes in rates can have a big effect on prices that buyers are willing -- or able -- to pay.

While rates remain low by historical standards, they already have risen significantly since this summer. Thirty-year fixed-rate mortgages now average 6.08%, after bottoming out at 5.31% in mid-June, according to HSH Associates, a financial publishers in Butler, N.J. The Mortgage Bankers Association expects rates to reach 7% in 2005.

Higher mortgage rates would impact the housing boom in a variety of ways. Higher rates make it harder for borrowers to qualify for a loan and reduce the size of the loan they can obtain. A borrower earning $50,000 could purchase a $195,000 home at 6% mortgage rates. But using the same lending standards, that borrower could only afford a $159,000 house if rates climbed to 8%.

Higher rates also mean bigger monthly payments. On a $350,000, 30-year fixed-rate mortgage, the monthly payment climbs by $470 to $2,568 when rates are at 8% instead of 6%.

So far, the impact of rising rates has been limited. Sales of existing homes surged in September, setting their third consecutive monthly record, according to the National Association of Realtors. Median home prices climbed 9.1% in September over a year earlier, according to the National Association of Realtors.

Still, if mortgage rates do climb to 7%, that's "high enough that it make a big difference in people's thinking," says Mark Zandi, chief economist at Economy.com in West Chester, Pa. The housing market is particularly vulnerable to rising rates, he adds, because so many people took advantage of low rates and bought homes earlier than they would have otherwise. Home-ownership climbed to record levels in the second quarter, according to the Commerce Department.

Federal banking regulators are already keeping a close eye on the potential risks. In a paper delivered to the Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. Board of Directors on Tuesday, FDIC economists warned that, "Home prices in less-affordable housing markets could be particularly vulnerable [to rising rates], since median family incomes are already straining to finance median priced homes."

Not everybody agrees with this warning. The same areas of the country that have high prices also have housing shortages, notes Lawrence Yun, a senior economist with National Association of Realtors. As a result, he predicts these regions won't be hurt more than other areas of the country by rising rates.

Other factors could blunt the damage, as well. A briskly growing economy could put more money in workers' wallets, providing a cushion against rising rates. In addition, as prices rise, more buyers are also likely to opt for adjustable-rate mortgages, which are tied to short-term rates that haven't yet moved upward. Adjustable-rate mortgages now account for 25.4% of mortgage loans, according to the Mortgage Bankers Association, up from 14.4% in mid-June. "It's a dandy shock absorber," says David Seiders, chief economist for the National Association of Home Builders.

But there are some signs the housing market could be cooling off. Housing affordability fell in the third quarter to its lowest level in a year, according to the National Association of Realtors. The Federal Reserve Board reported this week that demand for loans to purchase homes fell in the third quarter, the first drop in two years.

Home price growth has also been slowing. Home prices increased at an annualized rate of 3.13% in the second quarter, according to the Office of Federal Housing Enterprise Oversight, which regulates government-sponsored mortgage companies. That compares with an 8.77% rate in the year-earlier period, and is the lowest rate of increase since the fall of 1996.

For overheated markets, a slowdown in price appreciation would be a welcome breather. In some parts of the country, the buying frenzy has pushed prices out of line with local incomes. In California's Orange County, for instance, less than one in five households can now afford the median price house, according to the California Association of Realtors.

But when rates rise, home prices in such markets are likely to sink for several years or move sideways before they recover, according to Fidelity's analysis. In Orange County, for instance, 8% mortgage rates would push prices down 10% by 2006, according to the Fidelity study. In the center of the country, where prices never got so rich, rising rates are more likely to simply slow the rate price appreciation, not stop it.

Coastal markets also tend to be more sensitive to rising rates because the homes cost so much. In Los Angeles, where the median home price is $315,000, the monthly payment on a 30-year mortgage jumps by $423 if rates rise to 8% from 6%. In St. Louis, where the typical home goes for $117,000, that rise in rates would boost the monthly mortgage payment by just $157.

Louisiana-Pacific Corp. last year embarked on a restructuring program to shed traditional lines such as plywood to focus on more profitable businesses, such as manufacturing composite wood. The Portland, Ore., company has sold three of its 12 mills, shed 2,000 of its 10,000 jobs and is in the process of selling all 935,000 acres of its timberlands.

Other companies are faring better, despite the lumber glut. Weyerhaeuser and Plum Creek Timber Co. attributed their profits in the fourth quarter to their respective diversification strategies. Weyerhaeuser recently expanded its mix of mills by buying up rival Willamette Industries Inc. Plum Creek has a thriving real-estate business and timber holdings from coast to coast.
