Brownstones in Harlem To Blend With the Past
By RACHELLE GARBARINE

When Ruby Sprott read an advertisement last fall announcing plans for new two- and three-family houses in central Harlem for people with annual incomes of $50,000 to $70,950, she saw it as an opportunity to make the leap from a 12-year renter to a homeowner in the neighborhood.

Dr. Sprott, an education professor at the State University of New York at Old Westbury, is among the buyers at Lafayette Theater Townhouses, a $10.9 million development that will create 73 residences in 27 two- and three-family homes. Construction started last week on the houses, which will rise in clusters on nine vacant scattered sites bounded by West 129th and 134th Streets, between Adam Clayton Powell Jr. Boulevard and Fifth Avenue.

All the four-story homes, being built by the Novalex Contracting Corporation of Harlem, have been sold or are under contract to be sold at an average price of $327,694 since marketing began nine months ago. They are being designed to resemble the existing 19th-century brownstones being renovated on the blocks, which are in or near the historic Lafayette Theater area.

Charles A. Brass, president of the New York City Housing Partnership, an intermediary between the city and private builders, said the new houses would complete redevelopment of those blocks by filling in the vacant lots with housing that is affordable and blends with neighbors.

Both the area and the project are named for the theater that once stood at 2225 Adam Clayton Powell Jr. Boulevard, between 131st and 132nd Streets. From the time the theater opened in 1912 through the 1930's, it was the venue for various black theater companies, most known as the Lafayette Players. After being abandoned in the late 1930's, the theater was used temporarily as a church, and while being restored was destroyed by fire in 1968. A church now stands on the site.

When the Lafayette houses are completed in 18 months, Dr. Sprott will move into a new three-family home from a two-bedroom rental in the neighborhood. ''I never seriously looked to buy a house because I figured I couldn't afford one like this,'' she said. ''Now, I have more space in a house with appealing architecture that is new so I don't have to struggle with renovating an old one.''

Comparable new housing in the area starts at $450,000 to $550,000, said Carole N. Griffin, who heads the Griffin Group, a residential brokerage company in Harlem and is sales agent for Lafayette Theater Townhouses. The Black United Fund of New York is the community sponsor for the project, which received an $8 million construction loan from Fleet Bank, an active lender in Harlem.

Lafayette Theater Townhouses is part of the city's Neighborhood Builders Program, which helps neighborhood contractors develop and provide affordable housing. Its sponsors include the city's Department of Housing Preservation and Development and the Housing Partnership. Novalex has been involved for the last five years in the rehabilitation of about 90 buildings, mainly brownstones as well as small apartment houses, said Michael T. Rooney, its vice president. This is its first new construction project.

The state and the city provided capital subsidies averaging $23,900 per unit in each of the houses, and the Manhattan borough president's office has added $4,100 per unit. The total average subsidy for each home, $76,930, lowered the sale prices ranging from $279,900 to $359,900. Much of the money from the borough president's office will allow the developer to add such architectural details as stoops and cornices.

At the same time, under another city program, work is completed or will begin on three projects on and near the blocks of the Lafayette Theater Townhouses. They involve the renovation of 82 brownstones.

Lafayette Theater Townhouses was conceived four years ago. But it took until last month to secure the building permits and obtain the additional financing for the design of the homes, all the while keeping them affordable, said Carol L. Abrams, a spokeswoman for the housing department.

In addition to the public subsidies, another key is that the Federal National Mortgage Association, or Fannie Mae, is financing the mortgages under more flexible underwriting guidelines. These allow the lender to qualify buyers by deducting 60 to 75 percent of the rental income, for two- and three-family homes respectively, from the mortgage payment and to use a higher income ratio than the standard 28 percent.

The two-family homes, with 2,100 to 2,400 square feet, will have a 600-to-700-square-foot one-bedroom rental on the ground level, topped by the owner's three-bedroom unit with 1,500 to 1,600 square feet on three floors. The three-family homes, with 2,900 to 3,800 square feet, will contain an owner's unit, with 1,600 to 1,900 square feet on the first two floors. The larger homes will have two units that are two-bedroom rentals, with 900 to 950 square feet on the top two stories, while the smaller homes will have two one-bedroom units with 700 to 800 square feet on the upper floors.

Rents in Harlem have also risen to offset the monthly cost, Mr. Rooney of Novalex said. Monthly rents for the apartments will be $900 to $1,100 for the one-bedroom units and $1,100 to $1,300 for those with two bedrooms, Ms. Griffin, the broker, added. The project is also eligible for a 20-year tax abatement from the city.

The homes, designed by Roberta Washington Architects of Harlem, will have masonry concrete facades in addition to the cornices and stoops ''to replicate the look of original brownstones,'' Mr. Rooney said.

Landmark Status For Harlem Buildings
Metropolitan Desk; Section B
District Holds Hub of Black Culture
By NINA SIEGAL
1925 words
15 June 2000
The New York Times
NYTF
Page 1, Column 3
English
c. 2000 New York Times Company

Though their days were embittered by segregation and prejudice, African-Americans in Jazz Age New York always suspected that life was sweeter on Sugar Hill.

As they gazed up the hill from ''the valley,'' as central Harlem was sometimes called, Harlemites saw a shimmering bluff that would, from 1929 until midcentury, become a hub of elite black society. The names of those who lived there read like a Who's Who of black history: Ralph Ellison, Thurgood Marshall, Joe Louis, W. E. B. Du Bois, James Baldwin, Adam Clayton Powell Sr., Zora Neale Hurston, Duke Ellington and Paul Robeson, to name a few.

Now, some seven decades after the grand 19th-century row houses, brownstones and apartment buildings on Sugar Hill were first opened up to black renters and home buyers, the neighborhood is expected to become an official historic landmark on June 27, when the New York City Landmarks Preservation Commission designates a section as the Hamilton Heights/Sugar Hill Historic District.

This is the first phase of a process that will ultimately create a historic district encompassing almost 400 buildings from 145th Street to 155th Street, with jagged borders from Edgecombe Avenue to Amsterdam Avenue. The next phase is expected to begin in July, and the commission hopes the whole district will be designated as a landmark by June 2001.

One might expect that Mayor Rudolph W. Giuliani, who has said that he is looking to improve his relations with the city's minority residents, would be shouting from the rooftops about the landmark designation. Not so. In fact, the commission chairwoman he appointed in 1994, Jennifer J. Raab, who is widely credited with pushing through the designation after almost 15 years of inactivity on the issue, is reluctant to call the district a ''black district.''

''It has a phenomenal volume of perfectly preserved row houses,'' Ms. Raab said. ''They have their stoops, their ornaments, and it is remarkable in New York to find such a volume of intact architecture.''

But when asked whether she considers Hamilton Heights and Sugar Hill a black historic district, Ms. Raab said, ''I will have to think about that question.''

Landmark designation is always a subject of debates, and talking about race in any context is touchy. Landmarks officials seem to be particularly wary of combining the two. They may be sensitive, in part, because of previous accusations by some preservationists that the commission has neglected Harlem since it designated the Hamilton Heights Historic District, just south of the new district, in 1974.

Once the commission finally began carving out the new district in an area almost consecrated in black lore, the debates became more complicated: Should a historic district be created based on its architectural merits or on its cultural history?

This question is not unique to New York. In other cities where ''heritage tourism'' is encouraged in historic areas like Bronzeville in Chicago's South Side and Beale Street in Memphis, preservationists have also encountered difficulty in convincing officials to define these areas as ''black historic districts.''

Although Hamilton Heights/Sugar Hill will be one of the largest such districts in the nation when it is completed, it has not been a simple matter to trumpet it that way.

Most of the historic row houses on the hill were designed for rich white families at the turn of the century. It was not until the end of the 1920's -- late in the Harlem Renaissance -- that blacks were allowed to buy and rent the palatial homes.

''Whatever the commission calls it, there's no question that we're into a celebration of revolutionary terrain, because this is the central launching pad for black leadership in postwar America,'' said Carolyn Kent, a chairwoman of the landmarks committee for Community Board 9 in West Harlem, which encompasses the historic district.

Yet today, African-Americans are not the only ones living on the hill, as more homes are owned or rented by Hispanic, Asian, West African and white people.

''We look at it as a black district historically, but how can we say that now?'' said George Goodwill, chairman of the community board. ''It's tricky and it could become quite confrontational. I try to stay away from controversy and let the name Sugar Hill speak for itself.''

Julie Mayangar says she was the only white person living on West 147th Street between Convent and St. Nicholas Avenues in 1988, when she bought a four-story brownstone for $300,000. She knew something about the area's cultural history at the time, but moved in because of its well-preserved homes and the prevailing sense of neighborhood pride.

''For the architectural reasons we want to keep it that way, not just the cultural reasons,'' she said, adding that two mixed-race couples recently moved onto her block.

Since the Landmarks Preservation Commission was created in 1965, it has designated about 1,000 individual landmarks and about 70 historic districts. According to the commission's spokeswoman, Terri Rosen Deutsch, cultural factors ''drove the designations'' for a few sites in the city, like the recently designated Charlie Parker house on Avenue B. But, she added, ''I don't think there's any building that you can look at that we designated solely for cultural reasons. They all have some architectural merit.''

But there is an increasingly vocal group of preservationists throughout the city who would like to change that. One is Laura Hansen, a director of Place Matters, a joint project of the Municipal Art Society and City Lore, a cultural organization.

She said it is sometimes hard to make the argument for cultural designations. ''It's a lot easier to interpret how to regulate something with architectural details, simply because there's a lot of consensus in the architectural canon,'' she said. ''But with cultural history, there's a lot less agreement about what should be included.''

Yuien M. Chin, coordinator of the Hamilton Heights/Sugar Hill Historic District Committee, said Sugar Hill was a test case. ''It has presented a unique opportunity for the commission to really grapple with the idea of cultural landmarking,'' she said. ''It is an issue that is not unique to Harlem, but it's really important here because it is one of the largest African-American communities that has made such an impact on the history of its people, and it should be acknowledged as such.''

Some preservationists who argue this point say historic districts like Greenwich Village and South Street Seaport are de facto white historic districts.

A loose-knit group of Sugar Hill residents first introduced the idea of creating a landmark district there about 15 years ago, but the commission lost their documentation, Mr. Goodwill of the community board said. In 1994, when Ms. Raab became chairwoman, she announced that one of her top priorities was Harlem; three years later, a new group of residents formed a committee to push again for a historic district.

In the early planning stages, the commission focused on the exquisite row houses on the side streets, but was skeptical about including several large, more modern apartment buildings on Edgecombe Avenue. To some neighborhood historians, the idea of forsaking Edgecombe was unfathomable. The street sits on the cliff above central Harlem, and the long stretch of towering apartment buildings, many residents argued at commission hearings, were like the gates of the Emerald City.

''Edgecombe Avenue is synonymous with Sugar Hill,'' Ms. Chin said. ''It was the first place that people think of when you mention Sugar Hill.''

At first, the commission wanted to include three buildings on the avenue. But when neighborhood advocates argued for the cultural significance of the buildings, the commission added more, leading some preservationists, like Ms. Hansen, to think that the idea of designating cultural landmarks had gained some currency. In the end, eight Edgecombe buildings were included. But there are still two more that preservationists feel should be added: the Park Lincoln and the Florence Mills.

''These were the first buildings where African-Americans could rent on the hill,'' explained Michael Henry Adams, a preservationist. ''One of the things they did to attract black tenants was to give them welcoming names: Lincoln, the great liberator, and then Florence Mills, the performer who lived in Harlem.''

Ms. Raab said she is satisfied with the current district lines, but added, ''It's very much like other district processes; we will go back and look at the boundaries.''

Eleanor Eastman has lived on West 147th Street, in the center of the proposed district, since 1964. She and her former husband bought a four-story row house for just $18,500, in a neighborhood where similar homes have recently sold for $500,000 to $800,000. It is preserved almost exactly as it was 100 years ago. There is a turquoise stained-glass window between the grand staircase and the enormous dining room on the first floor; fireplaces inlaid with Italian marble are in every room; and there is a dumbwaiter to transport food from the downstairs kitchen.

When she moved in 36 years ago, she said, most of the neighboring homes had been chopped up into rooming houses, and gangs and crime plagued the area. But since the early 1990's she has seen many professionals return and begin restoring the properties.

''I think landmarking will help people have pride in what they have,'' Ms. Eastman said, ''and the services will get better because it will be a place the whole city can be proud of.''

But some people are dissatisfied.

Mr. Adams, the commission's most vocal critic, said that the landmarking was ''too little, too late'' because Harlem buildings like the original Cotton Club and the Harlem Opera House have already been torn down.

Others oppose the designation because they think it will limit their ability to change their homes. ''Landmarks wants to keep everything exactly as it used to be,'' Ms. Mayangar said. ''I think one can be a little eclectic and still keep the building tasteful and beautiful.''

But most of the neighborhood's residents are pleased and grateful.

''This was an effort that started 15 years ago and languished at the community board and at the commission for 15 years,'' Ms. Chin said. ''It was Jennifer Raab who finally pushed it through.''

Christabel Gough, secretary of the Society for the Architecture of the City, a nonprofit group that monitors the Landmarks Commission, said Harlem has long been an ''underrepresented area.''

''We're delighted to see that it's picking up again,'' she said. ''They're going to designate it on architectural grounds, but it just happens to be a place that is tremendously important in African-American history. So, why not call it that?''

A Westchester Builder Expands Into Connecticut
By ELEANOR CHARLES

THE Ginsburg Development Corporation, a 36-year-old home builder known for the quality of its houses and condominiums in Westchester County, has opened its first project in Connecticut, a condominium community of detached single-family homes in the Georgetown section of Redding.

Called Redding Woods, the Victorian cottage-style development at 62 Redding Road (Route 107) sits on a wooded knoll with views of forested hills receding into the distance. Of the 23 homes to be built by the end of the year, six have been sold since sales began a month ago. The four models, with 2,500 square feet to 3,600 square feet, are priced from $489,000 to $589,000.

Condos are the exception, not the rule in Redding, and Georgetown is the only section where they are allowed. Only one other condominium complex exists, with four apartments in a single building.

The Ginsburg company, which has filed plans for almost 400 homes in two projects elsewhere in Fairfield County, in Danbury and Newtown, has been known in Westchester for its focus on the high-end town-house niche, often on challenging sites.

Joseph A. Novella Jr., the president of Ginsburg Development CT in Danbury, the new Connecticut division set up by the company, said, ''Our charge is to become the premier multifamily community developer in Connecticut: to deliver what there is a dire lack of -- a variety of luxury town homes for empty nesters, aging baby boomers, singles, people who may not have children, people in nontraditional family situations, and do it at a reasonable price.''

Paul Shew, the village manager of Ossining, N.Y., spoke highly of the Ginsburg company. ''G.D.C. did a substantial project here known as Mystic Pointe -- high-end condos up to the $600,000 range,'' he said. ''Our building department was impressed with the fact that everything was done according to the book. No corners cut. The development is regarded as one of our better ones -- attractive, well built, exceptionally marketable.''

Paul Feiner, the supervisor of the Town of Greenburgh, N.Y., where the Ginsburg company has built three projects, has a fourth under construction and is planning a fifth, owns a Ginsburg home. ''Five years ago when my wife and I were looking for a house, the only three we liked were Ginsburg's. We bought one in Boulder Ridge from the original owner, and we love it. Ginsburg buys property nobody would think is buildable -- on a steep slope, or covered with rocks and boulders, and builds on it. The projects are very pleasing aesthetically and very well built.''

Thomas Gissen, Ginsburg's director of development, described its business philosophy like this: ''We have tried to develop a niche in the very upscale town home or clustered single-family home development, with heavy investment in architectural detail and landscaping. We look closely at the site, and the character of the local community. We don't try to jam a square peg into a round hole. Redding, for example, is very dissimilar from Yonkers. We try to work within that.''

BY making Redding the site of its first foray into Connecticut, the company is moving into an active market. Amy Pardee, the Redding's zoning enforcement officer, said: ''We're having a little building boom here. We're the next town up from lower Fairfield County, ripe for development, so we might as well brace ourselves.''

Incorporated in 1764, Redding has preserved much of its early rural atmosphere. It contains two districts listed on the National Register of Historic Places. Its large village green is the site of summer concerts, and its library, founded in 1908 by Mark Twain with a collection from his own library, holds one of the largest book sales in the state each August. Twain moved to Redding in 1908 and died there in 1910.

Only 9,000 people inhabit Redding's 32.3 square miles, almost all of it zoned for two-acre, single-family lots. More than 4,000 acres are protected in permanent open space. Georgetown is the only section of Redding with municipal sewers and water. Other parts of Georgetown belong to Ridgefield, Weston and Wilton.

Martin Ginsburg, the president and chief operating officer of the development company, and his brother, Sam, the other principal, are both architects, and their developments are known for their architectural integrity. Since 1964 the company, which is based in Hawthorne, N.Y., has built more than 1,650 houses and condominiums and 2,300 rental apartments in 20 projects along the East Coast, from Florida to Westchester. The brothers made a decision two years ago, Martin Ginsburg said, ''to go into new territories,'' specifically Connecticut.

Fourteen months ago, the Ginsburgs bought the 10-acre Redding tract for $1.5 million from Adam Lebarski, owner of the Georgetown Saloon on Main Street, and began reworking Mr. Lebarski's previously approved subdivision.

William Delsner, the designer of the Redding Woods homes, was brought into the project by Mr. Novella, whose family-owned construction company, the Novella Development Corporation in Danbury, merged with Ginsburg two years ago. Mr. Delsner, the Ginsburgs and Jane Didona, a landscape architect, scrapped the subdivision's original cookie-cutter layout and the identical designs for the houses, but the footprints of the houses could not be changed without going through a new approval process.

Mr. Delsner's designs incorporate a contemporary reinterpretation of turn-of-the-century details like bay windows, covered porches and trellises. The roof lines have several levels, the lower levels of the houses have stone facing, and the garage doors, with their arches and crisscrossed moldings, are reminiscent of carriage houses. Rather than being positioned in a straight line going up the hill, the houses are scattered informally along a single dead-end road.

Mr. Delsner is not a licensed architect, but a structural engineer on his staff and an architect sign off on the blueprints. On this project he worked closely with Ms. Didona to make the new houses look as if they had been there for years. Period street lights further that effect.

The site posed some challenges: one side drops to the road below at a 30-degree angle. ''Very often you get much more interesting results with a difficult site,'' Martin Ginsburg said. ''We've been dealing with difficult sites in Westchester for a long time, so we've developed a lot of expertise over the years.''

THE land was too steep for lawns, and the designers rejected a covering of loosely laid stone. Their solution? Huge sheets of green fabric installed against the hill, impregnated with wildflower seeds. With luck, each spring there will be little bluestem, switch grass, partridge pea, nodding bur marigold, showy tick trefoil and green-headed coneflower growing through the fabric, securing the soil, blooming and dying back naturally in the fall.

Of the four models of houses, three have three bedrooms and one has two. All have two and a half baths, two-car garages, nine-foot first-floor ceilings (eight feet upstairs) and solid-core interior doors. A large attic loft can be finished in one model for use as another bedroom or office. The space above the garage in another model can be finished as an all-purpose room with a pyramid ceiling 18 feet high at its point.

Basements will be finished, waterproofed and carpeted. Some will be completed as guest suites with living rooms, bedrooms and full baths. Basements in the houses with the steep backyards open onto patios.

The Ginsburg company has proposed two other developments that are currently going through the approval process in Connecticut: 280 town homes in Danbury and 110 condominiums for people 55 and older in Newtown.

Mr. Gissen, Ginsburg's director of development, said, ''We are hoping for another 'active adult' community of 70 luxury units in Farmington, 150 town homes in a lower Litchfield County town that I am not at liberty to name, and we are looking at properties in the New London and New Haven areas.''

When about two-thirds of the homes at Redding Woods are sold, a homeowners' association will be formed to maintain the road, lawns, landscaping and the exteriors of the houses. Common charges are expected to run about $2,000 a year. Property taxes, which will be set in July, may range from $5,500 to $6,500, said Diane Eppoliti, the sales agent for Redding Woods at Raveis Real Estate in Georgetown.

Florida Court Frees Retirement Community From School Levy

ORLANDO, Fla., May 19 -- The Florida Supreme Court has ruled that the developer of a retirement community does not have to pay fees imposed by the county to defray the cost of school construction.

Many communities across the country have imposed such one-time assessments, called impact fees, so that developments help pay for the additional services they require.

Fifteen Florida counties use the fees to raise about $71.4 million a year for new schools, according to the state's School Finance Construction Commission. State officials estimate that about $3 billion a year is needed for school construction.

The Florida Supreme Court ruled on Thursday that Aberdeen, a mobile home community in Ormond Beach, 40 miles north of Orlando, that prohibits children and requires residents to be 55 or older, did not have to pay the fees because it would not need any more schools.

The Volusia School District, of which Aberdeen is a part, must now pay back $90,000 in fees and interest to the retirement community. Aberdeen sued the school district in 1997, and a lower court judge ruled earlier this year that Aberdeen residents did not have to pay the fees.

The case has generated interest nationally among home builders, who have objected to impact fees for schools, utilities, roads and other services. In Florida, those fees add $4,000 to $5,000 to the cost of a new home, and Keith Hetrick, a lawyer representing the Florida Home Builders Association, said that ''prices people out of the market.''

California has the highest impact fees. In the Silicon Valley, the fees add up to $30,000 to the cost of a new home, Mr. Hetrick said.

Jay Shackford, a spokesman for the National Association of Home Builders in Washington, said impact fees are an ''unfair burden'' for first-time buyers because they pay for services and facilities that benefit the entire community.

Mr. Shackford said many states no longer imposed school construction fees on housing for older Americans.

''The ruling will have a critical financial impact on school districts that currently impose school impact fees,'' said Dick Graham, a lawyer representing the Volusia School District, ''and all other counties and school districts that may find the need to impose a school impact fee as a means of raising funds for new school construction.''

Wayne Blanton, executive director of the Florida School Boards Association, said counties had for several years had doubts about the reliability of the fees. This case may lead to eliminating them altogether, he said.

''For counties, they are going to have to look at every project that goes up in the future,'' Mr. Blanton said, ''and gauge whether it genuinely generates students.''

Mr. Hetrick said Aberdeen residents would still have to pay property taxes to the school district.

Bill Hall, superintendent of the school district, said the ruling separated the community.

''It allows a community of adults, basically, not to have anything to do with children,'' Mr. Hall said. ''That's potentially disastrous in this democratic society.''

WASHINGTON, May 16 -- Consumer prices were unchanged in April and the construction of new homes rose, government figures showed today, signaling that the economy was expanding with little to no inflation.

Declines in the cost of gasoline, clothing and air travel offset increases for medical care, food and housing last month, the Labor Department said. The flat Consumer Price Index came after a 0.7 percent jump in March and was the first report since last June's to show no change. The so-called core index, which excludes food and energy, rose 0.2 percent, half of March's 0.4 percent increase.

Housing starts rose 2.8 percent in April, to an annual pace of 1.663 million units, led by an increase in apartments and other multiple-unit developments, Commerce Department figures showed. That partly reversed March's 11.2 percent decline and put housing starts 6.5 percent higher than in the month a year earlier. At the same time, building permits fell, a sign of possible cooling ahead.

A separate Labor Department report showed that average weekly earnings adjusted for inflation rose 0.7 percent last month, after falling 0.5 percent in March. That is good news for home builders, which are counting on rising incomes and low unemployment to outweigh the effects of higher mortgage costs.

The consumer price report showed that retail prices increased 3 percent in the last 12 months. While that is less than the 3.7 percent annual rate in March, it exceeded the 2.7 percent increase for all of 1999.

Energy prices, which account for about a tenth of the Consumer Price Index, fell 1.9 percent in April after increasing 4.9 percent in March.

Prices are expected to start at $685,000, and each house will have 3,500 to 4,400 square feet. The 55-acre site, eight miles from the George Washington Bridge, was part of the adjacent Arcola Country Club in the 1940's. Later, it was privately owned and remained undeveloped.

Paramus is known more for the shopping malls within its borders than for high-end housing. But Ara Hovnanian, the company's president and chief executive, said he proceeded with the Fairway Oaks project because ''the market is on fire, and to get new housing this close to Manhattan is unusual and offered us a rare opportunity.''

The project represents part of a broadening of market focus by Hovnanian, which is best known as a builder of town houses for first-time buyers.

''We decided to dedicate a segment of our business to the luxury end because with the U.S. standard of living increasing, it makes sense for us to play in this arena,'' Mr. Hovnanian said.

That does not mean that ''we've abandoned selling $100,000 town houses,'' he said, adding that with a ''broad array of product, we can cover our bases.'' The company also has town houses on the Hudson River that sell for ''north of $1 million,'' he said, ''giving us comfort that we will do well in this market.''

At Fairway Oaks the company will offer six designs, each with four bedrooms and two-car garages. Some of the homes will have a large butler's pantry off the kitchen, porches or a bonus room over the garage. Buyers can also choose optional features like conservatories. Sales will start next month.

Location was also a factor in the decision by the Wharton Development Corporation of Manhattan to build 19 semicustom homes at the Enclave in Englewood Cliffs, about a mile from the George Washington Bridge. The 4,000-to-4,500-square-foot houses on quarter-acre lots are rising along Marietta Street, not far from Route 9W.

''The beauty of this project,'' said Peter C. Lewis, Wharton's president, ''is that we are creating a new neighborhood, which hasn't been done in this community in 30 years.''

He said in the year since sales started, base prices have risen from $850,000 to $950,000 and final prices, once buyers have customized their houses with upgraded moldings and appliances, have reached $1.2 million. So far, 15 houses have been sold to empty-nesters and move-up buyers from the Englewood Cliffs area and from Manhattan, he said.

As for the remaining four houses, Mr. Lewis said he is taking a ''build them and they will come'' approach and will construct them on speculation.

Another luxury project rising in Bergen County is Round Hill on Alford Drive in Saddle River, with 18 custom homes on a minimum of two acres. Most of the 50-acre wooded site was formerly an estate, with a century-old house and a horse barn that have been razed.

Pinnacle Custom, a division of Pinnacle Communities of Millburn, is Round Hill's developer. Prices begin at $2.3 million, with deed restrictions and architectural controls ''to protect buyers' interest and the project's integrity,'' said Michael Cantor, the director of construction.

The houses must be at least 7,000 square feet but no more than 10,000 square feet. And while buyers work with the company's architect in designing their houses, Pinnacle has the final say on the designs, Mr. Cantor said.

Sales began a year ago, and so far, eight houses have been sold, Mr. Cantor said. For such projects to succeed, he added, ''they have to be in the right location, so we limit our choices to unique properties in upscale communities.''

IN two months, the company expects to start a nine-home subdivision on a hilly 16-acre tract off Shrewsbury Road in Livingston in Essex County. The site was previously owned by former Gov. Thomas H. Kean, who sold it a year and a half ago. Mr. Kean, now the president of Drew University, lives in Far Hills, but his former hilltop house, which has been resold, has provided a name to the development, called Governor Hill at Livingston.

The new development will have architectural controls and restrictions similar to those in Saddle River. Lots will be an acre to one-and-a-half acres, and house prices will begin at $1.3 million, Mr. Cantor said.

In Manalapan in Monmouth County, Toll Brothers Inc. of Huntingdon Valley, Pa., which specializes in houses for move-up buyers, is developing 55 ''estate homes,'' each with four or five bedrooms and at least 4,300 square feet. The houses at Manalapan Hills, which is rising on a 120-acre former farm on Sweetman Lane, will have lots of 1.38 acres to 7 acres.

Sales began in January 1999, and so far 31 homes have been sold. Prices begin at $527,000, an increase of $81,000 since the start of the project, said Douglas Yearley, a vice president at Toll.

About half of the houses Toll has built in New Jersey in the last few years have been these large ''estate homes.'' ''It was not a concerted effort, but a response to market demand,'' Mr. Yearley said. ''We continue to be amazed at the depth and breadth of this market.''
